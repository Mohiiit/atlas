Module 02 Deep Dive: AMMs and Uniswap v4

Start from "how buying from a pool works" before advanced terms

First: What Is a Market Maker?

In old markets, you buy from another person. Market makers keep quotes so trading can happen quickly. In DeFi, AMM pools are automated market makers: the pool itself gives you price, and the price updates based on pool balance after each trade.

So when Maya "buys ETH from a pool," she is not buying from one specific seller. She is moving pool balances according to a formula.

Constant Product in Plain English

A simple pool follows x*y=k. Think of two buckets connected by a rule: if one bucket decreases, the other must increase enough to keep product constant. That is why bigger trades push price more. This is slippage.

Example: 1000 ETH and 2,000,000 USDC. Product is 2,000,000,000. Buying 10 ETH leaves 990 ETH. USDC side must become about 2,020,202. So buyer pays about 20,202 USDC before fee.

What Is an LP (Liquidity Provider)?

LPs are people like Maya who deposit assets into pool so others can trade. LP earns fees. But LP also carries inventory risk: if price trends strongly, LP may underperform simply holding assets. This is often called impermanent loss.

So LP return has two parts: fee income and inventory effect. Looking only at fee APR can be misleading.

Uniswap v2 -> v3 -> v4 (Simple Progression)

  • v2: one broad pool range, simple constant product.
  • v3: LP chooses price range. More capital efficiency, more active management.
  • v4: adds hooks (custom plugin logic around pool actions), singleton architecture, and flash accounting.

Important beginner interpretation: v4 is not "better price by magic." It is a framework where pool behavior can be customized, which brings both power and complexity.

What Is a Hook (No Jargon Version)

A hook is a custom function that runs before/after key actions like swap or liquidity update. Example: dynamic fee rule that raises fee during high volatility.

Think of hooks like optional extensions in software. Extensions can improve experience. They can also add bugs or security risks if poorly designed.

About "Aggregator" and "Intent Solver"

You flagged this correctly, so here is plain meaning:

  • Aggregator: app that checks many pools/exchanges and picks best route for your trade.
  • Intent Solver: system where user states desired outcome ("swap X for at least Y") and external solvers compete to fill it best.

You do not need these to understand AMM basics. They matter later because they decide where order flow goes.

First-Principles Questions for Uniswap v4

  1. What state changes before swap, during swap, and after swap?
  2. Can hook logic be manipulated by attackers?
  3. How does hook change LP's risk/reward compared to plain pool?
  4. If hook fails, does core accounting remain safe?
