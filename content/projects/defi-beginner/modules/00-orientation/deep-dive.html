Module 00 Deep Dive: How to Think in DeFi

Beginner-first, first-principles narrative

One Analogy We Will Reuse in Every Module

Meet Maya and Arjun. Maya has savings in ETH. Arjun wants to borrow funds to run a small business. In normal banking, a bank stands in the middle, decides who gets loans, and keeps ledgers privately. In DeFi, a smart contract is the ledger and rulebook. No bank manager. Rules are public.

This simple story is enough to understand almost all DeFi protocols: someone has capital, someone needs capital, and both need a trusted way to settle value transfers under risk.

What "First Principles" Means Here

First principles means we do not start from brand names, token tickers, or hype. We start from basic questions:

  1. What is the state?
  2. What actions can change state?
  3. What conditions must always remain true?
  4. Who earns money and who carries risk?
  5. What can go wrong first?

If you can answer these five for any protocol, you are no longer learning by memorization. You are reasoning.

State, Action, Invariant (Plain-English)

  • State: the current scoreboard. For Maya and Arjun, state includes Maya's deposit amount, Arjun's debt, and collateral values.
  • Action: what users can do, such as deposit, borrow, swap, repay.
  • Invariant: safety rule that should not break, such as "borrowed value must remain adequately backed by collateral."

Think of a board game: state is board position, actions are legal moves, invariant is "you cannot teleport pieces." DeFi is similar, except money is the piece.

Why Your Earlier Feedback Was Correct

If content uses terms like "aggregator" or "intent solver" before teaching what trading and pricing are, it blocks learning. In this revised curriculum, terms are introduced only after simpler concepts are stable.

Rule we follow from now on: no new jargon without a plain-language sentence and a small example.

Module Output You Should Produce

After each module, write a short memo with five lines:

  1. Protocol state variables
  2. Main user actions
  3. Core safety invariant
  4. Main revenue path
  5. Most likely failure path

This habit will build deep understanding faster than passive reading.
