Module 07: Risk, MEV, and Liquidations (Beginner Deep Dive)

Mode: primer | Running example: Maya's swap + Arjun's liquidation event

1. Why This Topic Exists

You can have perfect formulas and still produce bad outcomes if execution layer is adversarial. MEV and liquidation race dynamics determine who captures value and who absorbs losses in stress windows.

Analogy: emergency exit in stadium. Rules exist, but crowd dynamics decide whether people exit safely.

2. Terms Before Mechanics

  • MEV: profit from transaction ordering power.
  • Sandwich: attacker trades before/after victim to extract value.
  • Backrun: attacker captures predictable post-trade arbitrage.
  • Keeper: bot/operator executing liquidations or arbitrage actions.
  • Liquidation throughput: how much risky debt can be unwound per unit time.

3. Running Example Setup

Maya submits large swap. Arjun's leveraged position is near liquidation threshold. Network gets congested as volatility spikes.

4. Mechanism Walkthrough

  1. User transactions enter mempool.
  2. Searchers simulate profitable bundles.
  3. Builders/validators choose ordering.
  4. Liquidators race to close risky positions.
  5. Protocol solvency depends on whether risk-off actions clear fast enough.

5. Worked Example A (Swap Extraction)

Maya expects execution cost 20,000. In sandwich event, effective cost becomes 20,350. Extra 350 is extraction from ordering manipulation.

Even if pool math is correct, execution outcome for user worsens.

6. Worked Example B (Liquidation Capacity Stress)

System needs to liquidate 50M notional quickly. Normal keeper throughput = 8M per block equivalent. Congestion cuts it to 3M.

  • Risk backlog grows.
  • Positions continue deteriorating while waiting.
  • Bad debt probability rises sharply.

7. Misconception Correction

Misconception: “If liquidation logic exists, solvency is guaranteed.”

Correction: logic must execute in time. Throughput and competition matter as much as formula.

8. Failure Modes

  • Technical: keeper infrastructure outage.
  • Technical: oracle delay during fast market moves.
  • Economic: liquidation bonus too small for volatile conditions.
  • Market structure: concentrated block building worsens extraction behavior.

9. Protocol Evaluation Checklist

  1. What is measured liquidation throughput under stress tests?
  2. How often do liquidations fail or get delayed?
  3. Are there anti-sandwich or private-orderflow options for users?
  4. What fallback exists when mempool conditions degrade?

10. Check Your Understanding (With Answers)

  1. Can users lose from MEV without protocol bug?
    Yes, ordering manipulation can worsen execution even with correct contracts.
  2. Why is liquidation throughput crucial?
    It determines whether risk can be removed before collateral decays further.
  3. Is high liquidation bonus always good?
    No. Too high can hurt borrowers; too low can fail to attract keepers. Balance is needed.
