Module 05: Prediction Markets (Beginner Deep Dive)

Mode: primer | Running example: Maya buys YES shares

1. Why This Topic Exists

Prediction markets turn beliefs into tradable prices. Instead of asking “who sounds confident?”, you ask “who is willing to risk capital on a probability?”

Analogy: office pool before a sports final, but with continuous buy/sell and clearer payout rules.

2. Terms Before Details

  • YES/NO share: token paying 1 if outcome true/false respectively.
  • Implied probability: rough interpretation of YES price (e.g., 0.63 ≈ 63%).
  • Resolution source: authoritative data used to determine outcome.
  • Dispute window: period to challenge wrong resolution.
  • Liquidity depth: how much can trade without large price distortion.

3. Running Example Setup

Market question: “Will event X happen by date D?” YES trades at 0.63. Maya believes true probability is 70%.

4. Mechanism Walkthrough

  1. Market created with explicit question + resolution source.
  2. Traders buy/sell YES/NO, updating price.
  3. Event date passes.
  4. Outcome reported by oracle/resolver.
  5. Dispute period allows challenges.
  6. Final outcome set; winning side redeems at 1.

5. Worked Example A (Simple EV)

  • Maya buys 1,000 YES at 0.63 => cost 630.
  • If YES wins => payout 1,000 => gross profit 370.
  • If YES loses => loss 630.

Break-even probability before costs is about 63%.

6. Worked Example B (Liquidity Stress)

Suppose near expiry, order book is thin. Maya wants to buy 10,000 YES. First chunk fills near 0.63, but later chunks fill at 0.68 due to depth shortage.

  • Average entry maybe 0.665 instead of 0.63.
  • Edge shrinks materially.

Lesson: displayed price is not full story; execution quality matters.

7. Misconception Correction

Misconception: “YES price always equals true probability.”

Correction: price quality depends on liquidity, incentives, participant diversity, and resolution trust. Thin markets can be noisy.

8. Failure Modes

  • Question ambiguity: vague wording leads to disputes.
  • Resolver centralization: trust bottleneck if one actor controls outcome.
  • Late-stage manipulation: thin order books near closure.
  • Dispute economics broken: honest challengers cannot afford to challenge wrong outcomes.

9. Protocol Evaluation Checklist

  1. Is question objectively verifiable?
  2. Is resolution source public and durable?
  3. Is dispute process practical and fair?
  4. Is liquidity sufficient at relevant sizes?

10. Check Your Understanding (With Answers)

  1. What is the core trust anchor in prediction markets?
    Resolution and dispute mechanism, not just trading UI.
  2. Why can two markets with same headline event have different quality?
    Different wording, liquidity depth, and resolution design.
  3. If your model says 70% and market is 63%, are you guaranteed profit?
    No. Execution costs, slippage, and resolution risk can erase edge.
