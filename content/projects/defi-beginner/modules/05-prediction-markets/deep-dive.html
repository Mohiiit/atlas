Module 05 Deep Dive: Prediction Markets

How YES/NO prices work and where they fail

Plain-Language Start

A prediction market lets people buy/sell claims about future outcomes, such as "Will event X happen before date Y?" If YES share is 0.63, many read it as about 63% implied probability.

But that interpretation is only reliable when market is liquid and rules are clear.

Our Running Example

Maya thinks event chance is 70%. Market YES is 0.63. She buys 1,000 YES for 630.

  • If YES resolves true, payout is 1,000.
  • Gross profit is 370.
  • If YES resolves false, she loses 630.

Her edge depends on true probability estimate minus costs.

The Hard Part Is Not Trading, It Is Resolution

Most beginner confusion comes here: "Who decides what actually happened?" That is oracle/reporting + dispute process.

If question wording is vague, market can become untrustworthy even with high volume.

Good vs Bad Market Question

  • Good: "Will agency Z publish CPI above 3.0% for month M on official site by date D?"
  • Bad: "Will economy improve this quarter?"

First is verifiable. Second is subjective. Subjective questions produce dispute chaos.

Where Manipulation Can Appear

  1. Thin liquidity near market close.
  2. Ambiguous wording exploited in dispute process.
  3. Single trusted resolver with weak challenge path.

Beginner Evaluation Questions

  1. Can a random third person verify final outcome from public data?
  2. Is dispute process economically viable for honest challengers?
  3. How deep is liquidity near major price levels?
