Scalability

Even if a system is working reliably today, it doesn't mean it will cope with 10× more load tomorrow. Scalability is the term we use to describe a system's ability to cope with increased load. But scalability is not a one-dimensional label — you can't say "this system is scalable." The right question is: "If the system grows in this particular way, what are our options for coping with that growth?"

Describing Load

Before you can discuss performance, you need to describe the load on your system with a few numbers called load parameters. The choice of parameters depends on your system's architecture.

The load parameter that matters most depends heavily on what your system does. For social feeds, fan-out (how many people receive each post) is often the critical parameter — not raw request count.

Twitter's Home Timeline: A Case Study

Twitter's home timeline (showing tweets from people you follow) is one of the most instructive scaling examples in the book. The challenge: ~300 million users, ~500 million tweets/day, ~300k reads/second of home timelines. The bottleneck isn't writing tweets — it's reading home timelines. They tried two approaches.

Describing Performance

Once you've described load, you can ask: what happens to performance when load increases? You can look at this two ways: (1) increase load, keep resources fixed — how does performance degrade? (2) increase load — how much do you need to increase resources to keep performance constant?

For batch processing (Hadoop), the relevant metric is throughput — how many records per second, or how long it takes to run a job over a dataset. For online services, the relevant metric is response time — how long it takes to process a request and send a response.

Response Time vs Latency — Often Confused

Response time is what the client sees: time from sending a request to receiving a response. It includes service time, network delays, and queueing delays. Latency is the duration a request is waiting to be handled — the time spent latent, awaiting service. In practice, these terms are often used interchangeably but are technically distinct.

Percentiles: The Right Way to Measure

Response time varies for every request — it's not a single number, but a distribution. If you take the average, you lose critical information. The median (p50) tells you that half of requests are served faster than this time. But the median doesn't reveal the worst cases.

Why Tail Latency Matters More Than You Think

  • Valuable customers are disproportionately in the tail: the user with the most data (largest account) takes longest to serve — and they're often your most valuable customer
  • Fan-out amplifies tail latency: if a page requires 100 backend calls and you wait for all of them (parallel fan-out), the page time is determined by the slowest call. p99 of one call becomes p63 of the page — even a small tail affects most page loads
  • SLAs are written in percentiles: "p99 response time must be under 1 second" — the median is irrelevant for SLAs

With 100 parallel calls, each at p99 = 1s, the probability that at least one is slow is 1 - (0.99)^100 ≈ 63%. So the page is slow 63% of the time even though individual backends are only slow 1% of the time.

Head-of-Line Blocking

A small number of slow requests can hold up the processing of subsequent requests — called head-of-line blocking. A server processing 5 requests in parallel gets one slow one; even if new requests arrive instantly, the slow one occupies a worker slot, increasing queuing time for everything behind it. This is why percentile measurements must be taken at the client side, not just at the server — server-side measurements miss queueing delays.

Clients should use a rolling window of the last 10 minutes (or last N requests) to measure response time distributions, not just instantaneous samples.

There Is No Universal Scalable Architecture

An architecture that scales well for one application may be totally wrong for another at the same scale. A system handling 100k RPS of 1KB requests looks very different from a system handling 3 RPS of 2GB requests, even though both process 100MB/s. The architecture must be built around the specific load parameters of the application.

Stateless services (that read from databases and don't hold state themselves) are easy to scale horizontally. Stateful systems are much harder — adding nodes introduces data distribution, consistency, and coordination problems that scale out complexity enormously. For this reason, the common wisdom is: scale up until you can't, then scale out. The former is simpler and more appropriate than most engineers realize.

"The architecture of systems that operate at large scale is usually highly specific to the application — there is no such thing as a generic scalable architecture. The problem may be the volume of reads, the volume of writes, the volume of data to store, the complexity of the data, the response time requirements, the access patterns, or usually some mixture of all of these plus many more issues." — Kleppmann, DDIA
