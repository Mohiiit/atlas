Unit 5

The Satellite Link Budget

The link budget is the single most important calculation in satellite communications. It is a methodical accounting of every gain and every loss between the transmitter and the receiver, expressed in decibels. If you understand one calculation in satcom, make it this one.

Learning Objectives

  • Calculate EIRP from transmit power, antenna gain, and feed loss
  • Derive Free Space Path Loss and explain why it varies with elevation angle
  • Account for atmospheric attenuation, rain fade, and polarization loss
  • Compute receiver G/T and understand system noise temperature
  • Assemble the complete link budget equation and compute C/N
  • Apply SNR penalties and determine link margin
  • Read and construct a waterfall chart for visual link budget analysis

1. What Is a Link Budget?

A link budget is a bookkeeping exercise. You start with the power leaving the transmitter and then add every gain (antenna gain, coding gain) and subtract every loss (path loss, atmospheric loss, cable loss) until you arrive at the signal-to-noise ratio at the receiver. Every term is in decibels, so the entire calculation is addition and subtraction.

The chain looks like this:

C/N = (Transmit Power + Gains) - (Losses) + (Receiver Figure of Merit) - (Noise Reference)

This is THE central calculation in satcom because it answers the only question that matters: Does the link close? If C/N exceeds the required threshold, data gets through. If not, the link fails. Every design decision in a satellite system -- antenna size, transmit power, orbit altitude, modulation scheme -- ultimately flows through the link budget.

Why decibels? Because multiplication becomes addition. A signal passing through an amplifier (gain) and then a lossy cable (loss) involves multiplying power ratios. In dB, you simply add the gain and subtract the loss. This makes the entire link budget a single column of additions.

2. EIRP -- Effective Isotropic Radiated Power

EIRP represents how much power an isotropic (omnidirectional) antenna would need to radiate to produce the same signal strength in the direction of the receiver. It bundles the transmitter power, antenna gain, and feed losses into a single number.

EIRP = Ptx + Gtx - Lfeed   [dBW]

The unit is dBW (decibels relative to 1 Watt). Using dBW rather than dBm avoids confusion in link budgets since all standard reference tables use Watts.

For a Starlink satellite at Ku-band: Ptx ~ 10 W (+10 dBW), Gtx ~ 38 dBi peak, Lfeed ~ 1 dB. This yields EIRP ~ 47 dBW at boresight.

Notice: EIRP is a direction-dependent quantity. The 47 dBW above is at boresight. As the satellite scans its beam off-axis, the antenna gain drops and so does EIRP. At a 60-degree scan angle, you might lose 3-4 dB of gain, reducing EIRP to ~43 dBW.

3. Free Space Path Loss

Free Space Path Loss (FSPL) represents the spreading of electromagnetic energy as it propagates through a vacuum. It is not absorption -- it is geometry. The power per unit area decreases with the square of the distance.

FSPL = 20 · log10(4π · d · f / c)   [dB]

Where d is distance in meters, f is frequency in Hz, and c is speed of light (3 × 108 m/s).

Concrete Example: Starlink at 12 GHz, 550 km

At zenith (satellite directly overhead): d = 550 km = 5.5 × 105 m

FSPL = 20 * log10(4 * pi * 5.5e5 * 12e9 / 3e8)
     = 20 * log10(4 * pi * 2.2e7)
     = 20 * log10(2.765e8)
     = 20 * 8.442
     = 168.8 dB  (~169 dB)

At the horizon (elevation ~ 25 degrees): d ~ 1100 km. The FSPL becomes ~175 dB, roughly 6 dB more loss. At very low elevations (10 degrees), the slant range stretches further, and FSPL can reach ~180 dB. The total variation across a pass is roughly 11 dB.

4. Atmospheric Attenuation

The signal must pass through Earth's atmosphere. At Ku-band (12 GHz), the atmosphere absorbs and scatters energy. The loss depends on the path length through the atmosphere, which is a function of elevation angle.

Latmo = Lzenith / sin(elevation)

This is the ITU-R P.676 "cosecant law" model. At zenith, Lzenith is roughly 0.3 dB at 12 GHz in clear sky. But at 10-degree elevation, the path slices through ~5.7x more atmosphere:

L_atmo(90deg) = 0.3 / sin(90) = 0.3 dB
L_atmo(45deg) = 0.3 / sin(45) = 0.42 dB
L_atmo(25deg) = 0.3 / sin(25) = 0.71 dB
L_atmo(10deg) = 0.3 / sin(10) = 1.73 dB

Rain Fade

Rain is the dominant atmospheric impairment at Ku-band. Raindrops absorb and scatter microwave energy. The ITU-R P.618 rain model uses:

  • Rain rate: mm/hr exceeded for a given percentage of time
  • Specific attenuation: dB/km at the operating frequency
  • Effective path length: geometric path through the rain cell

Typical values at 12 GHz: light rain (5 mm/hr) adds ~1 dB, moderate rain (25 mm/hr) adds ~4 dB, heavy rain (50 mm/hr) adds 8+ dB. Rain fade is the largest single variable in a Ku-band link budget and the primary reason LEO systems include generous link margin.

5. Polarization Loss

Starlink uses circular polarization. The ground terminal uses a linearly polarized antenna (phased array with electronic polarization synthesis). The mismatch between transmitted and received polarization introduces loss.

Key Concepts

  • Axial ratio: A perfectly circular wave has AR = 0 dB. Practical antennas might have AR = 1-2 dB.
  • Cross-pol isolation: How well the antenna rejects the opposite polarization. Typical: 25-30 dB.
  • Polarization mismatch loss: With circular TX and linear RX, the theoretical loss is 3 dB (you capture only one linear component). Starlink ground terminals synthesize circular from linear elements, reducing this to ~0.3-0.5 dB.

Scan-angle degradation: As the phased array scans off boresight, the element patterns become asymmetric. The circular polarization purity degrades, increasing polarization loss. At 60-degree scan, the additional polarization loss can be 0.5-1.0 dB.

In the link budget, we use L_pol ~ 0.5 dB nominal, increasing at low elevation angles.

6. Receiver G/T -- Figure of Merit

G/T (pronounced "G over T") is the receiver's figure of merit. It captures both the receive antenna gain and the system noise in a single number.

G/T = Grx - 10 · log10(Tsys)   [dB/K]

System Noise Temperature

The system noise temperature T_sys is the sum of all noise sources referred to the antenna port:

Tsys = Tant + Tlna + Tcable
ComponentTypical ValueNotes
Tant (antenna noise)40-200 KVaries strongly with elevation
Tlna (LNA noise)75 KNF ~1 dB
Tcable (cable/feed)15 KShort cable, low loss

Why Tant varies with elevation: When the antenna points at zenith, it "sees" cold sky (~20-40 K). When it points near the horizon, the antenna sidelobes pick up thermal radiation from the warm ground (~290 K). This ground noise spillover can increase Tant by 100-150 K at low elevations.

Example G/T Calculation

At 45-degree elevation: Grx = 33 dBi, Tsys = 140 K

G/T = 33 - 10*log10(140)
    = 33 - 21.5
    = 11.5 dB/K

At 10-degree elevation: Grx = 28 dBi (scan loss), Tsys = 280 K (ground noise)

G/T = 28 - 10*log10(280)
    = 28 - 24.5
    = 3.5 dB/K

That is an 8 dB swing in G/T between zenith and low elevation. This is why low-elevation performance is so challenging.

7. The Complete Link Budget Equation

Now we assemble every piece into the complete carrier-to-noise ratio:

C/N = EIRP - FSPL - Latmo - Lrain - Lpol + G/T - kB - 10·log10(BW)

Where kB = Boltzmann's constant = -228.6 dBW/K/Hz, and BW is the noise bandwidth in Hz.

Worked Example: Starlink Downlink at 45-degree Elevation (Clear Sky)

ParameterValueRunning Total
EIRP (Ptx + Gtx - Lfeed)+47.0 dBW+47.0
Free Space Path Loss-172.0 dB-125.0
Atmospheric attenuation-0.42 dB-125.4
Rain loss (clear sky)-0.0 dB-125.4
Polarization loss-0.4 dB-125.8
Receiver G/T+11.5 dB/K-114.3
Boltzmann's constant+228.6 dBW/K/Hz+114.3
Bandwidth (240 MHz)-83.8 dB-Hz+30.5

Result: C/N = 30.5 dB -- this is a very healthy link. It supports high-order modulation (64QAM) with plenty of margin.

Same Pass, 10-degree Elevation

ParameterChange from 45 degValue
EIRP-3 dB (scan loss)+44.0 dBW
FSPL-6 dB (more distance)-178.0 dB

Result: C/N ~ 12.7 dB -- dropped by 17.8 dB from the 45-degree case. Now only QPSK with low code rate is viable.

8. SNR Penalties

The theoretical C/N is not the whole story. Real implementations suffer additional losses that degrade the effective SNR:

PenaltyTypical ValueCause
EVM Penalty0.5 - 1.5 dBError Vector Magnitude: DAC/ADC quantization, IQ imbalance, phase noise in the oscillator
Doppler Tracking Penalty0.2 - 0.8 dBResidual frequency error from tracking loop lag. Worse near horizon where Doppler rate is highest.
Implementation Loss1.0 - 2.0 dBCatch-all: channel estimation errors, timing recovery, interleaver depth limits
effective_SNR = C/N - penalty_evm - penalty_doppler - penalty_impl

For our 45-degree example: effective_SNR = 30.5 - 0.8 - 0.3 - 1.5 = 27.9 dB

For the 10-degree case: effective_SNR = 12.7 - 1.2 - 0.7 - 1.5 = 9.3 dB

9. Link Margin

The link margin is the difference between what you have and what you need:

margin = effective_SNR - required_SNR

The required_SNR comes from the chosen modulation and coding scheme (ModCod). For example:

  • QPSK 1/2: requires ~3.5 dB
  • QPSK 3/4: requires ~6.5 dB
  • 16QAM 3/4: requires ~13.5 dB
  • 64QAM 3/4: requires ~19.5 dB

Positive margin = link works. The bigger the margin, the more resilient the link is to unexpected impairments (rain, interference, degraded hardware).

At 0 dB margin: The link is right at the edge. A puff of cloud, a slight temperature increase in the LNA, or a momentary pointing error will cause errors. Adaptive modulation systems will drop to a lower ModCod to regain margin.

Negative margin: The link fails. Bit errors exceed the FEC correction capability. Packets are lost.

Example Margins

At 45 degrees (effective_SNR = 27.9 dB, using 64QAM 3/4, required 19.5 dB):

margin = 27.9 - 19.5 = +8.4 dB  -- Healthy margin

At 10 degrees (effective_SNR = 9.3 dB, using QPSK 3/4, required 6.5 dB):

margin = 9.3 - 6.5 = +2.8 dB  -- Tight but workable

At 10 degrees in rain (effective_SNR = 5.3 dB, still QPSK 3/4):

margin = 5.3 - 6.5 = -1.2 dB  -- LINK FAILS, must drop to QPSK 1/2

10. Waterfall Chart

A waterfall chart visualizes the link budget as a cascade. You start with EIRP and watch the signal level rise with gains and fall with losses until you reach the final C/N.

The waterfall chart immediately reveals the dominant terms: FSPL is the overwhelming loss, and the Boltzmann constant term (which converts from power to noise-referenced units) is the corresponding large gain. The smaller losses (atmospheric, polarization) are almost invisible at this scale -- but they matter at low elevation where every dB counts.

11. Code Connection

The link budget calculation lives in the simulator at:

  • src/simulation/link-budget/link-budget.ts -- The main link budget computation. Computes EIRP, FSPL, atmospheric losses, and assembles C/N. Called every simulation tick with the current satellite state.
  • src/simulation/link-budget/doppler.ts -- Doppler shift and Doppler rate calculations. Feeds the tracking loop penalty into the SNR computation.
  • src/simulation/link-budget/tracking-loop.ts -- Models the PLL/FLL tracking loop that compensates for Doppler. Outputs residual frequency error that becomes the Doppler tracking penalty.
