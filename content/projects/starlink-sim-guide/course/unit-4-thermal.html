Unit 04

Spacecraft Thermal Management

How heat flows in the vacuum of space, why temperature limits performance, and how a 5-node lumped thermal model drives the simulator's PA throttling and oscillator drift.

Learning Objectives

  • Explain why heat transfer in space is fundamentally different from terrestrial environments
  • Identify and quantify the major heat sources on a LEO communications satellite
  • Apply the Stefan-Boltzmann law to calculate radiative heat rejection and understand the T^4 nonlinearity
  • Construct a lumped thermal node model with thermal mass, conductance, and heat generation
  • Derive and integrate the thermal ODE using forward Euler to predict temperature evolution
  • Trace how PA junction temperature drives thermal throttling and degrades link performance

1 Why Thermal Is Different in Space

On Earth, three mechanisms transfer heat: conduction (through solids), convection (through moving fluids), and radiation (electromagnetic emission). In the vacuum of space, there is no air or fluid, so convection does not exist. Heat can only move by conduction within the spacecraft structure and by radiation to the surrounding environment.

This creates a unique challenge. On Earth, a hot component simply heats the air around it, which rises and carries heat away (natural convection). You can add a fan for forced convection. In space, none of this works. A hot power amplifier die must conduct its heat through a thermal path to a radiator surface, which then emits infrared radiation into the void. The efficiency of this radiative cooling depends on the fourth power of the temperature -- a strongly nonlinear relationship that makes thermal design one of the most demanding aspects of spacecraft engineering.

The Hot Side / Cold Side Problem

A satellite in LEO has one side bathed in direct sunlight (~1361 W/m^2 of solar flux) and the opposite side facing the cold of deep space (~3 K). The sun-facing side can reach over 100 degrees C, while the space-facing side can drop below -100 degrees C. All electronics must operate within their rated temperature range (typically -40 to +85 degrees C), so the thermal control system must manage this enormous gradient.

Adding to the complexity: the satellite orbits the Earth every ~94 minutes. For roughly a third of each orbit, the satellite passes through Earth's shadow (eclipse). During eclipse, solar input drops to zero instantly, and the spacecraft begins cooling. When it exits eclipse, the solar flux returns abruptly. This thermal cycling creates stress on materials and forces temperature swings that the electronics must tolerate.

The thermal environment in numbers: Direct solar flux: 1361 W/m^2. Earth infrared: ~237 W/m^2. Earth albedo: ~400 W/m^2 (reflected sunlight). Deep space temperature: 3 K (cosmic microwave background). Eclipse duration: ~35 minutes out of a ~94-minute orbit for a 550 km, 53 degree inclination orbit.

2 Heat Sources

Heat enters the spacecraft from two categories: external (solar loading) and internal (electronics dissipation). Both must be rejected through the thermal control system.

Solar Loading

The solar constant at Earth's distance is 1361 W/m^2. The actual heat absorbed by a surface depends on its absorptivity (alpha) and the projected area facing the sun. In the simulator, the default solar loading is split: approximately 100 W to the radiator and 50 W to the array panel, for a total of 150 W during sunlit portions of the orbit. During eclipse, solar loading drops to zero.

Electronics Dissipation

Every watt of electrical power consumed by the spacecraft eventually becomes heat. The dominant heat sources are:

Digital electronicsFPGAs, processors, memory -- all dissipate power as switching losses and leakage. Typically the largest total heat source on the satellite.Digital Board

The PA thermal challenge: The power amplifier generates the highest heat flux density on the satellite. While total digital electronics dissipation may be higher in watts, the PA concentrates its heat in a tiny GaN die (~10 mm^2). This makes the PA junction the thermal bottleneck -- it reaches dangerous temperatures long before any other component. Thermal throttling of the PA is the primary failure mode in the simulator.

3 Radiative Heat Rejection

In the vacuum of space, the only way to reject heat is by thermal radiation. Every surface above absolute zero emits electromagnetic radiation according to the Stefan-Boltzmann law:

Q_rad = epsilon * sigma * A * (T^4 - T_env^4)

where epsilon is the surface emissivity (0 to 1), sigma = 5.67e-8 W/m^2/K^4 is the Stefan-Boltzmann constant, A is the radiating area in m^2, T is the surface temperature in Kelvin, and T_env is the effective environment temperature in Kelvin.

Why T^4 Matters

The fourth-power dependence on temperature is the most important feature of radiative cooling. It means:

  • At low temperatures, radiative cooling is very weak. A surface at 0 degrees C (273 K) radiates much less than one at 100 degrees C (373 K).
  • At high temperatures, cooling becomes extremely effective. A small temperature increase produces a large increase in radiated power.
  • The system is self-regulating: if a radiator heats up, it radiates more power, which tends to stabilize the temperature. This negative feedback is why radiators work without active control.

Concrete example: a 2 m^2 radiator with emissivity 0.85, viewing 50% deep space (3 K) and 50% Earth (255 K):

Radiator TempT (K)Q to space (W)Q to Earth (W)Total Q_rad (W)
0 degrees C273264221485
25 degrees C298353309662
50 degrees C323465421886
75 degrees C3486025581160

Notice: going from 25 to 50 degrees C (a 25 K increase) adds 224 W of cooling capacity. Going from 50 to 75 degrees C (same 25 K increase) adds 274 W. The hotter the radiator runs, the more effectively it dumps heat -- but of course, hotter radiator temperatures mean hotter electronics temperatures, which is exactly what we are trying to avoid.

Figure 1: Spacecraft thermal energy balance. Heat enters from solar flux and electronics dissipation. Heat leaves only by radiation from the radiator surface to space and Earth. At steady state, input equals output.

View Factors

The radiator does not see 100% cold space. Part of its field of view is filled by Earth, which is a warm body at ~255 K. The view factor describes the fraction of the radiator's hemisphere occupied by each thermal sink. In the simulator: 50% space view (T_env = 3 K) and 50% Earth view (T_env = 255 K). The net radiated power is the sum of both contributions.

Because Earth is at 255 K, the effective cooling for heat radiated "toward Earth" is much weaker than heat radiated toward deep space. A radiator at 300 K radiating toward 3 K space sees a temperature difference of 297 K, but toward 255 K Earth, the difference is only 45 K. The T^4 makes this asymmetry even more extreme in terms of power.

4 Lumped Thermal Models

A detailed thermal analysis of a satellite might involve finite-element models with millions of nodes. For a real-time simulator that runs at 1-second resolution, we use a much simpler approach: the lumped parameter (or "lumped capacitance") model.

The idea is to discretize the spacecraft into a small number of thermal nodes, each representing a physically distinct region with a single representative temperature. Each node has three properties:

  • Temperature T (degrees C): The current temperature of the node.
  • Thermal mass C (J/degrees C): How much energy is required to change the node's temperature by 1 degree. High thermal mass means the node responds slowly to heat input (it is "thermally sluggish").
  • Heat generation Q (W): Internal heat produced by electronics within this node.

Nodes are connected to each other by thermal conductances G (W/degrees C), which represent the ease of heat flow between them. Heat flows from hot nodes to cold nodes proportionally to the temperature difference: Q_flow = G * (T_hot - T_cold).

Analogy to electrical circuits: A lumped thermal model is exactly analogous to an RC circuit. Temperature is voltage, heat flow is current, thermal mass is capacitance, thermal conductance is 1/resistance. The thermal ODE is the same as the capacitor charging equation. Engineers who understand RC circuits already understand thermal modeling -- it is the same math.

The Thermal-Electrical Analogy in Detail

The analogy breaks down for radiation, which is strongly nonlinear (T^4). There is no common electrical component with a V^4 characteristic. This nonlinearity is what makes spacecraft thermal analysis more challenging than simple RC circuit analysis. The simulator handles it by evaluating the radiative term at each time step using the current temperature, then treating it as a constant heat sink for that step (explicit linearization).

When Lumped Models Fail

The lumped parameter approach works when the temperature gradient within each node is small compared to the gradient between nodes. This is quantified by the Biot number: Bi = h*L/k, where h is the heat transfer coefficient, L is the characteristic length, and k is the thermal conductivity. For Bi << 1, the lumped model is valid.

For our PA junction node: the GaN die is small (~3 mm) and highly conductive, so the temperature is nearly uniform across the die. Bi << 1 -- the lumped model is excellent. For the array panel: it is large (~1 m) with modest conductivity (aluminum), but the heat input is distributed. The lumped model is approximate but acceptable for a real-time simulator. A detailed thermal model might use 100+ nodes for the array panel alone, but this would be computationally expensive and not necessary for understanding the system-level behavior.

Thermal Resistance Networks

The conductance values in the simulator encode the combined thermal resistance of the physical heat path. For example, the PA-to-Array conductance of G = 2.0 W/C represents:

  • Junction-to-case resistance of the GaN die (~5 C/W)
  • Thermal interface material (TIM) between the package and the mounting pad (~2 C/W)
  • Spreading resistance in the array panel structure (~3 C/W)

Total resistance: 5 + 2 + 3 = 10 C/W, giving G = 1/R = 0.1 W/C per die. With multiple PA dies (or accounting for the fact that the simulator uses a simplified model), the aggregate conductance is approximately 2.0 W/C.

Figure 2: The 5-node lumped thermal model. Each node has a thermal mass (C) and initial temperature. Conductances (G) govern heat flow between nodes. The radiator is the only node that rejects heat to the external environment via radiation.

Why These Specific Values?

PA Junction (C = 5): The GaN die is tiny (~a few mm^2). It has almost no thermal mass, so it heats up and cools down in seconds. This is why the PA junction is always the first thing to overheat.

Radiator (C = 500): The radiator is a large aluminum panel, typically 2 m^2. Its enormous thermal mass means it changes temperature very slowly -- it acts as a thermal flywheel that smooths out transient heat pulses from the electronics.

The G = 2.0 bottleneck (PA to Array): The conductance from the PA junction to the array panel is only 2.0 W/degrees C. This modest thermal coupling is the bottleneck that causes the PA junction to run hot. If you could increase this conductance (better thermal interface, heat pipes), the PA would run cooler. This is the single most impactful parameter in the thermal model.

6 The Thermal ODE

Each thermal node obeys an energy balance equation. The temperature rate of change is determined by the heat flowing in (generation + conduction from hotter neighbors) minus heat flowing out (conduction to cooler neighbors + radiation):

C_i * dT_i/dt = Q_gen_i + Sum_j[ G_ij * (T_j - T_i) ] - Q_rad_i

where C_i is the thermal mass of node i, Q_gen_i is the internal heat generation, G_ij is the conductance between nodes i and j, and Q_rad_i is the radiative heat loss (nonzero only for the radiator node).

Forward Euler Integration

The simulator integrates this ODE using the simplest possible method: forward Euler with a time step of dt = 1 second.

T_i(t + dt) = T_i(t) + (dT_i/dt) * dt

For each simulation tick:

  1. Compute heat generation: Distribute electrical loads from the power bus to the thermal nodes. PA heat comes from the PA model; other loads are distributed by fixed fractions (e.g., 85% of array electronics heat goes to the Array Panel, 15% to Digital Board).
  2. Add solar loading: If not in eclipse, add solar heat to the Radiator (100 W) and Array Panel (50 W).
  3. Compute conductive flows: For each conductance pair, compute Q = G * (T_j - T_i) and add/subtract from each node.
  4. Compute radiation: For the Radiator node only, compute Q_rad using Stefan-Boltzmann with the 50/50 space/Earth view factors.
  5. Divide by thermal mass: Convert the net heat rate (W) to temperature rate (degrees C/s) by dividing by C.
  6. Update temperatures: T_new = T_old + dT/dt * dt.
Why Euler is sufficient here: Forward Euler is normally considered inaccurate and potentially unstable for stiff ODEs. However, our thermal model has moderate thermal masses (5-500 J/C) and conductances (1-5 W/C), giving time constants of 1-250 seconds. With dt = 1 s, the fastest node (PA Junction: tau = C/G = 5/2 = 2.5 s) requires a Courant-like condition dt < 2*tau = 5 s, which is satisfied. The error from Euler is small compared to the model's inherent approximations (lumped nodes, simplified geometry).

7 Thermal Throttling

The PA junction temperature is the critical thermal metric. When it rises too high, the semiconductor material degrades and the device can be permanently damaged. The simulator implements a two-level throttling scheme:

ConditionThresholdActionLink Impact
NormalT_PA < 85 CNone -- full power operationNo degradation
Mild throttle85 C <= T_PA < 95 CExtra 2 dB output backoff~2 dB less EIRP. Modulation may downshift. Throughput drops ~20-30%.
Severe throttleT_PA >= 95 CMaximum backoff; PA near shutdownSevere EIRP reduction. Link may fail at low elevation. Throughput drops 50%+.
Thermal throttling is the primary failure mode: In the simulator, the most common reason for a pass to underperform is not rain fade or interference -- it is PA junction overheating triggering thermal throttling. The PA generates ~4 W of heat through a conductance of only 2.0 W/C to the array panel. If solar loading is high or the radiator efficiency is degraded (from faults), the PA junction temperature climbs relentlessly. At 85 C, the system throttles back, reducing transmitted power. This cascades through the entire link budget: lower EIRP means lower C/N, which means lower-order modulation, which means lower throughput.

The Throttling Cascade

The thermal throttling creates a causal chain that spans the entire simulator:

  1. PA junction temperature rises above 85 C
  2. Thermal model sets throttling = "mild", adding 2 dB of output backoff
  3. RF chain model reduces PA output power by 2 dB
  4. Link budget computes lower EIRP, resulting in lower C/N at the receiver
  5. Modulation model selects a lower-order ModCod (e.g., drops from 16QAM 3/4 to QPSK 1/2)
  6. Spectral efficiency drops from 3.0 bit/s/Hz to 1.0 bit/s/Hz
  7. Throughput drops by ~67%

This is a real example of the cross-layer causality that the simulator is designed to demonstrate: a thermal event (junction overheating) cascades through RF, link budget, modulation, and protocol layers to produce a throughput impact.

8 Temperature-Performance Mapping

Beyond the binary throttling thresholds, temperature continuously affects component performance through analog derating effects:

PA Derating

GaN power amplifiers lose performance as temperature rises above the reference temperature (25 degrees C):

ParameterDerating RateAt T = 75 C (delta = 50 C)
P1dB (compression point)-0.02 dB/C-1.0 dB -- the PA saturates 1 dB earlier
Small-signal gain-0.015 dB/C-0.75 dB -- less amplification
Efficiency-0.003 per C-0.15 -- more waste heat (positive feedback!)
Thermal positive feedback loop: Higher temperature reduces PA efficiency, which means more waste heat for the same RF output, which further raises the temperature. This positive feedback can cause thermal runaway if the cooling capacity is insufficient. The radiator's T^4 law provides negative feedback that usually dominates, but a degraded radiator or excessive solar loading can tip the balance.

Oscillator Drift

The TCXO (Temperature-Compensated Crystal Oscillator) frequency drifts with temperature:

delta_f / f = alpha * (T - T_ref) + beta * (T - T_ref)^2

where alpha = 0.5 ppm/C and beta = 0.01 ppm/C^2. At the oscillator's nominal 42 C (delta = 17 C above 25 C reference): drift = 0.5 * 17 + 0.01 * 289 = 8.5 + 2.89 = 11.4 ppm. At 12 GHz, this is 136 kHz of frequency error -- enough to require the receiver's carrier tracking loop to compensate, but well within its capture range.

At 80 C (delta = 55 C): drift = 0.5 * 55 + 0.01 * 3025 = 27.5 + 30.25 = 57.75 ppm = 693 kHz at 12 GHz. This approaches the PLL unlock threshold. Above 80 C, the oscillator may lose lock entirely, causing signal dropout regardless of link budget margin.

The Design Temperature Budget

Spacecraft thermal engineers allocate a "temperature budget" to each component, specifying the maximum allowable temperature under worst-case conditions (end of life, maximum solar loading, highest power mode). For the PA junction, a typical budget might look like:

ContributionTemperature Rise (C)
Base plate (radiator average)30
Conduction through structure to array panel+10
Conduction from array panel to PA package+15
Junction-to-case thermal resistance+20
Total junction temperature75 C (nominal)
End-of-life degradation margin+10
Worst case85 C = throttle threshold

This budget shows why the 85 C throttle threshold exists: it represents the junction temperature at which the thermal design margin is completely consumed. Running hotter than 85 C means operating outside the qualified temperature range, risking accelerated aging and potential device failure.

Arrhenius Acceleration and Component Lifetime

The lifetime of semiconductor devices decreases exponentially with temperature, following the Arrhenius equation:

Lifetime factor = exp( E_a / k_B * (1/T_2 - 1/T_1) )

where E_a is the activation energy of the dominant failure mechanism and k_B is Boltzmann's constant. A commonly cited rule of thumb for GaN devices: every 10 C increase in junction temperature halves the device lifetime.

At 75 C junction (nominal design), a GaN PA might have a mean time to failure (MTTF) of 10 million hours (~1140 years). At 85 C: MTTF drops to 5 million hours. At 95 C: 2.5 million hours. At 105 C: 1.25 million hours (~143 years). Even at elevated temperatures, the absolute MTTF is long -- but a satellite constellation with thousands of PAs will see statistical failures much sooner than the mean.

This is another reason for thermal throttling: not just immediate performance, but long-term reliability. Running the PA at 95 C for even a short time accumulates damage that shortens the satellite's useful life.

Heat Pipes and Advanced Thermal Control

Real satellite thermal control systems often include heat pipes -- sealed tubes containing a two-phase working fluid that efficiently transports heat over long distances with very low temperature drop. A heat pipe can have an effective thermal conductivity thousands of times higher than solid copper.

In the simulator's 5-node model, heat pipes are implicitly represented by the conductance values. The G = 5.0 W/C conductance from the Array Panel to the Radiator is higher than it would be for simple aluminum conduction, representing the use of embedded heat pipes in the array structure. If a thermal fault degrades this conductance (simulating a heat pipe dryout, for example), the entire thermal balance shifts and the PA junction heats up more rapidly.

Other thermal control technologies used in real spacecraft but not explicitly modeled in the simulator include:

  • Multi-layer insulation (MLI): Blankets of aluminized Mylar that reduce radiative heat exchange between spacecraft surfaces and the environment. Effective emissivity can be as low as 0.01.
  • Louvers: Mechanical shutters over radiator surfaces that open when the spacecraft is warm and close when cold, regulating the effective emissivity.
  • Phase change materials (PCM): Substances that absorb heat by melting (storing latent heat) during peak power periods and release it when power drops. Acts as thermal capacitance.
  • Thermoelectric coolers (TEC): Peltier devices that actively pump heat from sensitive components (like the oscillator) to maintain a stable temperature.

9 Eclipse Transitions

When the satellite enters Earth's shadow, solar loading drops to zero essentially instantaneously (within a few seconds as the terminator sweeps past). The thermal response is immediate but the node temperatures respond at different rates:

  • Radiator (C = 500): Begins cooling slowly as it still radiates to space but receives no solar input. Temperature might drop 0.1-0.2 C per minute.
  • Array Panel (C = 200): Loses its 50 W of solar input. Cools more noticeably, perhaps 0.3 C per minute.
  • PA Junction (C = 5): If the PA is still transmitting, its temperature is dominated by self-heating, not solar loading. It actually benefits from eclipse because the array panel (its heat sink) is now cooler.
  • Oscillator (C = 10): Responds quickly to changes in the digital board temperature. May see a few-degree drop during a 35-minute eclipse.

Eclipse exit is more abrupt and more stressful: the full solar flux hits the spacecraft suddenly, and the radiator and array panel begin absorbing 150 W that was not there a second ago. The thermal transient from eclipse exit is typically the time when temperatures are most likely to spike and approach throttling thresholds.

Eclipse Frequency and Duration

For the Starlink orbit (550 km altitude, 53 degrees inclination), the eclipse fraction varies with the angle between the orbital plane and the sun vector. At certain times of year, the orbital plane can be nearly perpendicular to the sun line, meaning every orbit passes through Earth's shadow. At other times, the satellite may be in continuous sunlight.

Maximum eclipse duration occurs when the satellite passes through the center of Earth's shadow. For 550 km altitude:

Eclipse half-angle: alpha = arcsin(R_E / (R_E + h)) = arcsin(6371/6921) = 66.8 deg
Maximum eclipse fraction: 2 * alpha / 360 = 133.6 / 360 = 37.1%
Maximum eclipse duration: 0.371 * 93.8 min = ~34.8 minutes

During the worst-case eclipse, the spacecraft loses all 150 W of solar input for 35 minutes, then regains it abruptly. The thermal model must handle these transients without allowing temperatures to exceed limits in either direction -- not just overheating, but also under-cooling (some components have minimum operating temperatures).

Thermal Inertia as a Design Tool

The large thermal mass of the radiator (C = 500 J/C) is not an accident -- it is a deliberate design choice. During eclipse, the radiator continues radiating to space even though solar input has stopped. Its temperature drops slowly thanks to its high thermal mass. When the sun returns, the radiator absorbs the solar pulse gradually. Without this thermal inertia, temperature swings would be much more severe.

Engineers sometimes add dedicated thermal mass (phase change materials, thermal storage units) specifically to buffer transient heat loads. The principle is the same as a capacitor in an electrical circuit: absorb short-duration spikes so that the steady-state system can handle the average load.

Multi-Orbit Thermal Evolution

Over multiple orbits, the thermal state reaches a periodic steady state -- temperatures oscillate around an average value with each eclipse cycle. The PA junction, with its tiny thermal mass, closely tracks the fast variations. The radiator, with its massive thermal inertia, barely fluctuates. The time to reach periodic steady state depends on the slowest time constant in the system: for the radiator, tau = C/G_total is on the order of 100 seconds, but the radiative cooling introduces nonlinearity that can extend settling to 30-60 minutes (several orbits).

The simulator typically runs for a single pass (~8 minutes), which is short enough that orbital thermal cycling is not a major factor. However, the initial temperatures are set to represent a satellite that has already reached periodic steady state during a sunlit phase -- hence the relatively warm initial values (30-55 degrees C).

10 Code Connection

The thermal subsystem is implemented in src/simulation/thermal/thermal-model.ts. It is a single, self-contained file of about 200 lines.

Node Constants

// Node indices
const PA = 0, ARRAY = 1, DIGITAL = 2, OSC = 3, RADIATOR = 4;

// Thermal masses (J/C)
const THERMAL_MASS = [5, 200, 50, 10, 500];

// Conductances: [nodeA, nodeB, G in W/C]
const CONDUCTANCES = [
  [PA, ARRAY, 2.0],
  [ARRAY, RADIATOR, 5.0],
  [DIGITAL, RADIATOR, 3.0],
  [OSC, DIGITAL, 1.5],
  [DIGITAL, ARRAY, 1.0],
];

createInitialThermalState()

Returns the initial temperatures for all five nodes, plus flags for eclipse status and throttling level.

stepThermal()

The core integration function. Called once per simulation tick with the previous thermal state, PA heat dissipation (from the PA model), electrical loads (from the power bus), and solar load (0 if in eclipse). Implements the full Euler step described in Section 6:

// Conductive coupling between nodes
for (const [a, b, G] of CONDUCTANCES) {
  const flow = G * (T[b] - T[a]);
  dTdt[a] += flow;
  dTdt[b] -= flow;
}

// Radiative cooling (only radiator node)
const Q_rad = computeRadiativeHeat(T[RADIATOR]);
dTdt[RADIATOR] -= Q_rad;

// Euler integration
for (let i = 0; i < NUM_NODES; i++) {
  newT[i] = T[i] + (dTdt[i] / THERMAL_MASS[i]) * dt_s;
}

computeRadiativeHeat()

Implements the Stefan-Boltzmann law with split view factors:

function computeRadiativeHeat(temp_C: number): number {
  const T_K = temp_C + 273.15;
  const qSpace = eps * sigma * A * spaceViewF * (T_K**4 - 3**4);
  const qEarth = eps * sigma * A * earthViewF * (T_K**4 - 255**4);
  return qSpace + qEarth;
}

12 Self-Assessment Questions

Q1: A spacecraft radiator has area 2 m^2, emissivity 0.85, and operates at 50 C. Calculate the total radiated power, assuming 50% view factor to space (3 K) and 50% to Earth (255 K). Show the T^4 computation explicitly.

T_rad = 50 + 273.15 = 323.15 K. T_rad^4 = 323.15^4 = 1.091e10 K^4.
To space: Q_space = 0.85 * 5.67e-8 * 2.0 * 0.5 * (1.091e10 - 3^4) = 0.85 * 5.67e-8 * 1.0 * (1.091e10 - 81) = 4.82e-8 * 1.091e10 = ~526 W.
To Earth: T_earth^4 = 255^4 = 4.228e9. Q_earth = 4.82e-8 * (1.091e10 - 4.228e9) = 4.82e-8 * 6.682e9 = ~322 W.
Total: ~848 W. Notice that the space-facing view radiates 63% more power than the Earth-facing view, even though they have the same area and emissivity. This is entirely due to the temperature difference (T_env = 3 K vs 255 K) appearing as T_env^4.

Q2: The PA junction has thermal mass C = 5 J/C and conductance G = 2.0 W/C to the array panel. If the PA dissipates 4 W and the array panel is at a constant 40 C, what is the steady-state junction temperature? How long does it take to reach 63% of that equilibrium?

Steady state: At equilibrium, dT/dt = 0, so Q_gen = G * (T_PA - T_array). 4 = 2.0 * (T_PA - 40). T_PA = 40 + 4/2.0 = 42 C. Wait -- that seems low, and it is: this assumes the array panel stays at exactly 40 C (infinite thermal mass). In reality the array panel also heats up.
If the PA starts at 40 C (same as the array), the time constant is tau = C/G = 5/2.0 = 2.5 seconds. After one time constant (2.5 s), the junction reaches 63% of the way to equilibrium: T = 40 + 0.63 * 2 = 41.26 C.
The small time constant (2.5 s) is the key insight -- the PA junction responds to heat input in seconds, not minutes. This is why it is the thermal bottleneck.

Q3: During an eclipse (35 minutes), the solar loading drops from 150 W to 0 W. Which thermal node cools the fastest in degrees per minute, and which cools the slowest? Why?

The rate of cooling depends on: (a) how much solar heat the node was receiving, and (b) its thermal mass. The Array Panel loses 50 W of direct solar input but has C = 200 J/C, so the initial cooling rate from lost solar alone is 50/200 = 0.25 C/s = 15 C/min -- but this is offset by conductive heat input from the PA and Digital Board, so actual cooling is slower (~0.3-0.5 C/min).
The Radiator loses 100 W of solar input with C = 500 J/C: 100/500 = 0.2 C/s = 12 C/min initially, but it also loses heat from reduced conductive input as other nodes cool. Actual cooling is ~0.1-0.2 C/min because the radiator was already rejecting much of this heat anyway.
Fastest cooling: Array Panel (most solar loading relative to its mass). Slowest: Radiator (huge thermal mass, and it was already radiating most of the solar heat away).

Q4: Explain the thermal positive feedback loop in the PA. Under what conditions does it lead to thermal runaway, and what prevents it in normal operation?

The loop: higher T_PA reduces PA efficiency (by 0.003 per C above 25 C). Lower efficiency means more waste heat for the same RF output: if efficiency drops from 45% to 40%, waste heat rises from 3.9 W to 4.8 W. This extra heat raises T_PA further, which reduces efficiency further, and so on.
What prevents runaway: The radiator's T^4 law provides negative feedback that grows faster than the positive feedback. As the radiator heats up, its cooling power increases as T^4, which is much stronger than the linear efficiency degradation. The equilibrium point is where the radiator's increased cooling matches the PA's increased waste heat.
Runaway conditions: If the radiator is degraded (e.g., reduced emissivity from micrometeorite damage, or a thermal fault reducing the radiator efficiency factor), the negative feedback weakens. If simultaneously the PA is running at high power (high solar elevation, demanding link budget), the positive feedback can dominate, and the PA temperature ramps up until severe throttling forces the system to reduce transmit power -- the "thermal runaway arrest" by throttling.

Q5: The simulator uses forward Euler with dt = 1 s. The PA junction has C = 5 J/C and the largest conductance connected to it is G = 2.0 W/C. Is the Euler integration stable? What would happen if C were reduced to 1 J/C?

The stability criterion for forward Euler on dT/dt = -(G/C)*T + ... is dt < 2*C/G_total, where G_total is the sum of all conductances connected to the node. For the PA junction: G_total = 2.0 W/C (only one connection). Stability limit: dt < 2 * 5 / 2.0 = 5.0 seconds. Since dt = 1 s < 5 s, the integration is stable.
If C were reduced to 1 J/C: stability limit = 2 * 1 / 2.0 = 1.0 seconds. At dt = 1 s, we are exactly at the stability boundary -- the integration would oscillate and could blow up due to floating-point rounding. We would need to either reduce dt to 0.5 s or use an implicit integration method. This illustrates why the PA junction's small thermal mass is computationally challenging, not just physically challenging.

Q6: The PA junction hits 85 C and triggers mild thermal throttling (extra 2 dB backoff). Trace the complete causal chain from this thermal event to the change in user throughput. How many subsystem models are involved?

The chain involves 5 subsystem models:
1. Thermal model: T_PA reaches 85 C, sets throttling = "mild".
2. RF Chain model: Reads throttling flag, applies 2 dB extra output backoff. PA output drops from, say, 31 dBm to 29 dBm.
3. Link Budget model: EIRP drops by 2 dB. Combined with path loss, atmospheric loss, and receiver G/T, the C/N (carrier-to-noise ratio) at the ground receiver drops by 2 dB.
4. Modulation model: The adaptive ModCod selector sees 2 dB less C/N. It may downshift from 16QAM 3/4 (3.0 bit/s/Hz) to 8PSK 2/3 (2.0 bit/s/Hz) or even QPSK 1/2 (1.0 bit/s/Hz), depending on the margin.
5. Protocol model: Lower spectral efficiency means fewer bits per second. With 250 MHz bandwidth and overhead, throughput might drop from 600 Mbps to 400 Mbps or less.
This cascade -- thermal to RF to link to modulation to protocol -- is exactly the "hardware-first" philosophy the simulator is built to demonstrate.
