Unit 01

Orbital Mechanics & Satellite Passes

How satellites orbit Earth, what TLEs encode, how SGP4 propagation works, and why LEO passes last only minutes. The foundation for every downstream subsystem in the simulator.

Learning Objectives

  • Read and decode every field in a Two-Line Element (TLE) set
  • Explain what SGP4 propagation does and why it accounts for J2 and atmospheric drag
  • Define elevation, azimuth, and slant range in the context of a ground pass
  • Derive the Doppler shift formula and predict frequency behavior through a pass
  • Distinguish AOS, TCA, and LOS and explain how the simulator computes each
  • Estimate the duration of a LEO pass from orbital velocity and visible arc geometry

1 What Is a TLE?

A Two-Line Element set is a compact, standardized format for describing a satellite's orbit. NORAD (North American Aerospace Defense Command) assigns every tracked object a catalog number and publishes updated TLEs through sources like CelesTrak. The format was designed in the 1960s for punch-card era computing and has persisted because it pairs directly with the SGP4 propagation algorithm.

A TLE encodes six Keplerian orbital elements plus perturbation terms. It is not a snapshot of position and velocity; it is a set of mean orbital elements that, when fed into SGP4, produce position and velocity at any future time.

The Six Keplerian Elements

The TLE encodes six orbital elements that, together, completely specify an orbit and the satellite's position within it. Understanding these elements is essential for interpreting TLE data:

ElementSymbolWhat It Defines
Semi-major axis (a)Derived from mean motionThe size of the orbit. Not stored directly; computed from mean motion via Kepler's third law: a = (mu / n^2)^(1/3).
Eccentricity (e)eThe shape of the orbit. e = 0 is a perfect circle; 0 < e < 1 is an ellipse; e = 1 is parabolic (escape).
Inclination (i)iThe tilt of the orbital plane relative to the equator. 0 deg = equatorial; 90 deg = polar; 53 deg for Starlink.
RAAN (Omega)OmegaThe orientation of the orbital plane in space. Specifies where the ascending node (northbound equator crossing) is.
Argument of Perigee (omega)omegaThe orientation of the ellipse within the orbital plane. Where the lowest point (perigee) is located.
Mean Anomaly (M)MWhere the satellite is along its orbit at epoch. 0 deg = at perigee. Increases uniformly with time for a Keplerian orbit.

Three elements (a, e, omega) define the shape and orientation of the ellipse. Two elements (i, Omega) define the orientation of the orbital plane. One element (M) defines the satellite's position within the orbit. Together, they are sufficient to compute the satellite's position and velocity at any time, assuming a two-body (Keplerian) orbit.

From Mean Motion to Altitude

The TLE gives mean motion n in revolutions per day, but we often want the altitude in kilometers. The conversion uses Kepler's third law:

n_rad = n_rev * 2 * pi / 86400 (convert to rad/s)
a = (mu / n_rad^2)^(1/3) (semi-major axis in km)
altitude = a - R_E (subtract Earth's radius)

For STARLINK-1008: n = 15.345 rev/day = 1.112e-3 rad/s. a = (398600 / (1.112e-3)^2)^(1/3) = (3.222e11)^(1/3) = 6858 km. Altitude = 6858 - 6371 = 487 km. Wait -- this is lower than the expected 550 km! The discrepancy arises because "mean motion" in a TLE is a mean value that includes the J2 perturbation correction. The actual osculating semi-major axis fluctuates around this mean. The simulator uses SGP4 to handle this correctly; the raw calculation above is only approximate.

2 SGP4 Propagation

A TLE by itself only tells you the orbit at one instant (the epoch). To predict where a satellite will be at any future time, you need a propagator -- an algorithm that integrates the equations of motion forward (or backward) in time. SGP4 (Simplified General Perturbations, version 4) is the standard propagator designed to work with TLE data.

What SGP4 Actually Does

At its core, SGP4 starts with the six mean orbital elements from the TLE and applies a series of analytical corrections to account for perturbations that cause the real orbit to deviate from a pure Keplerian ellipse. The output at any requested time is a position vector (x, y, z) and velocity vector (vx, vy, vz) in the Earth-Centered Inertial (ECI) coordinate frame.

The key perturbations SGP4 models are:

PerturbationCauseEffect
J2 oblatenessEarth is not a perfect sphere; it bulges at the equatorCauses RAAN to precess (~7 deg/day for LEO), argument of perigee to rotate, and mean motion to shift. This is by far the dominant perturbation.
J3, J4 harmonicsHigher-order gravitational asymmetries (pear shape, etc.)Small corrections to the J2 effects. Important for precision but secondary.
Atmospheric dragResidual atmosphere at LEO altitudes (even at 550 km)Removes energy from the orbit, lowering altitude over time. Encoded in the B* term. More significant during solar maximum when the thermosphere expands.
Solar/lunar gravityGravitational pull from Sun and MoonCauses long-period oscillations in orbital elements. More important for higher orbits (GEO, etc.).
Why TLEs go stale: SGP4 is an analytical approximation, not a numerical integration. It uses simplified perturbation models that accumulate error over time. A typical LEO TLE is accurate to ~1 km within a few days of epoch, but can be off by tens of kilometers after a week. NORAD publishes fresh TLEs every few hours to compensate. In the simulator, we use a recently fetched TLE and propagate only over a single pass (~10 minutes), so the error is negligible.

SGP4 vs. Numerical Propagation

Why not use a more accurate method, like a numerical integrator (Runge-Kutta 4, for example) that directly integrates the equations of motion with a high-fidelity gravity model? There are two reasons:

  1. TLEs are tied to SGP4. The mean elements in a TLE were generated by NORAD using the inverse of the SGP4 theory. They are only correct when used with SGP4. Feeding them into a different propagator would introduce systematic errors from the mismatch between the generation and propagation models.
  2. Speed. SGP4 is an analytical closed-form solution -- it evaluates a set of trigonometric expressions directly, without iteration. It can compute thousands of positions per second. A numerical integrator must take many small time steps, making it orders of magnitude slower. For a real-time simulator that needs position at every second, SGP4's speed is essential.

High-precision propagation (using numerical integration with detailed force models) is used for orbit determination and maneuver planning, where accuracy of meters matters. For our purposes -- determining whether a satellite is visible from a ground station and computing the approximate look angles -- SGP4 accuracy of ~1 km is more than sufficient.

The satellite.js Library

The simulator uses the satellite.js open-source library, which is a JavaScript/TypeScript port of the original FORTRAN SGP4 implementation. It provides:

Worked Example: TLE Epoch Decoding

The epoch field in our TLE is 26101.18331139. Let us decode this step by step:

  1. Year: First two digits = 26. Since 26 < 57, this is 2000 + 26 = 2026. (The 57 threshold distinguishes 1957-1999 from 2000-2056.)
  2. Day of year: Remaining digits = 101.18331139. The integer part (101) is the day number: April 11, 2026.
  3. Fractional day: 0.18331139 * 24 = 4.3995 hours. That is 4 hours, 23 minutes, 58 seconds UTC.

The epoch is: 2026-04-11 at 04:23:58 UTC. This is the reference time for all the orbital elements. The simulator's parseTleEpoch() function performs exactly this calculation.

Orbital Period and Repeat Ground Track

With a mean motion of 15.345 rev/day, the satellite completes one orbit in 93.82 minutes. Since the Earth rotates 360 degrees in 1436.07 minutes (one sidereal day), the ground track shifts westward by:

Ground track shift = 360 / (1436.07 / 93.82) = 360 / 15.31 = 23.5 deg per orbit

After 15.31 orbits (about 23.9 hours), the ground track almost -- but not quite -- repeats. The slight mismatch means the satellite covers the entire latitude band over many days. For a constellation, this ensures that every ground point sees multiple satellite passes per day, though not at the same time.

For a single satellite like STARLINK-1008, a ground station at 47.67 N latitude (Redmond, WA) typically sees 2-4 usable passes per day, with maximum elevations varying from 25 degrees (marginal) to occasionally 80+ degrees (excellent). The simulator's findBestPass() searches 48 hours by default to find the highest of these passes.

The getOrbitalPeriodMinutes() Utility

The simulator provides a helper function for converting the internal satellite.js mean motion (stored in radians per minute) to a human-readable orbital period:

export function getOrbitalPeriodMinutes(satrec: SatRec): number {
  // satrec.no is mean motion in radians/minute
  // Period = 2*pi / no
  return (2 * Math.PI) / satrec.no;
}

This is used by the pass-finder to estimate the search duration: three orbital periods (~285 minutes) guarantees at least one complete visibility window. The search then extends to 48 hours (via findBestPass()) to find the optimal pass geometry.

The J2 Perturbation in Detail

The J2 zonal harmonic is the single most important perturbation for LEO satellites. Earth's equatorial radius is about 21 km larger than its polar radius. This mass distribution creates a gravitational potential that is not purely 1/r, and the extra term causes three observable effects:

Nodal precession rate: dOmega/dt = -(3/2) n J2 (R_E / a)^2 cos(i) / (1-e^2)^2

For STARLINK-1008 at 53 degrees inclination: the RAAN (ascending node) drifts about -5.1 degrees per day westward. This is not a defect -- it is a design feature. By choosing the right inclination, satellite constellation designers can arrange for the orbital planes to precess at a rate that maintains even coverage.

3 Pass Geometry

When a LEO satellite comes within line-of-sight of a ground station, we call this a pass. The geometry of a pass is described by three angles and one distance, all measured from the ground station's perspective.

Elevation, Azimuth, and Slant Range

Elevation is the angle above the local horizon, measured in degrees. 0 degrees is on the horizon; 90 degrees is straight overhead (zenith). The minimum usable elevation for satellite communications is typically 25 degrees (the elevation mask) because at lower angles, path loss increases dramatically, atmospheric attenuation is worse, and terrain/buildings block the signal.

Azimuth is the compass bearing to the satellite, measured clockwise from true north. 0/360 degrees = north, 90 degrees = east, 180 degrees = south, 270 degrees = west. The azimuth changes continuously as the satellite moves across the sky.

Slant range is the straight-line distance from the ground station to the satellite, in kilometers. It is shortest at the point of closest approach (near TCA) and longest at AOS and LOS when the satellite is near the horizon.

Figure 1: Pass geometry showing elevation angle, slant range, and the relationship between ground station and satellite positions.

Computing Slant Range from Elevation

Given the satellite altitude h and the elevation angle el, the slant range can be computed using the law of cosines on the triangle formed by the Earth's center, the ground station, and the satellite:

d = R_E [ sqrt( (h/R_E + 1)^2 - cos^2(el) ) - sin(el) ]

where R_E = 6371 km is Earth's radius. At zenith (el = 90 degrees), d equals h = 550 km. At the elevation mask (el = 25 degrees), d grows to about 1100 km. This factor-of-two range difference has direct consequences for the link budget -- free-space path loss increases as d^2.

4 AOS, TCA, and LOS

Every satellite pass is characterized by three critical moments:

AOS (Acquisition of Signal): The instant when the satellite's elevation first rises above the elevation mask (25 degrees in our simulator). This is when the link can first be established. In the simulator, this is found by a coarse scan at 30-second intervals followed by refinement to 1-second precision.

TCA (Time of Closest Approach): The moment when the satellite reaches its maximum elevation. This is not necessarily directly overhead -- for most passes, the maximum elevation is well below 90 degrees. TCA is the best moment for communications because slant range is minimized and atmospheric path length is shortest.

LOS (Loss of Signal): The instant when the satellite's elevation drops back below the elevation mask. The link is terminated and the pass is complete.

Figure 2: Elevation curve through a high-elevation pass showing AOS, TCA, and LOS. The shaded region is the usable pass window above the 25 degree elevation mask.

How the Simulator Finds a Pass

The pass-finding algorithm in pass-finder.ts uses a two-phase approach:

  1. Coarse scan: Propagate the satellite position at 30-second intervals over three orbital periods (~285 minutes). At each step, compute the elevation angle. When the elevation first crosses above the mask, mark a candidate AOS. When it drops below, mark a candidate LOS.
  2. Refinement: Go back to the neighborhood of the coarse AOS and LOS, and scan at 1-second intervals to find the precise crossing times. Then generate second-by-second geometry for the entire pass.

The findBestPass() function extends this by searching up to 48 hours of orbital data and returning the pass with the highest maximum elevation, which gives the best link quality.

5 The Doppler Effect

As a satellite approaches a ground station, the received frequency is higher than the transmitted frequency. As it recedes, the frequency is lower. This is the Doppler effect, and for a 12 GHz Ku-band signal with a satellite moving at 7.5 km/s, the shift can be significant.

The Doppler Formula

f_received = f_carrier * (1 - v_radial / c)
delta_f = -f_carrier * v_radial / c

where v_radial is the radial component of the satellite's velocity (the component along the line of sight from the ground station to the satellite). The sign convention: v_radial is negative when the satellite is approaching (range decreasing) and positive when receding (range increasing).

At 12 GHz with a maximum radial velocity of ~7.0 km/s (near AOS/LOS when the satellite is nearly tangent to the horizon):

delta_f_max = 12e9 * 7000 / 3e8 = 280 kHz

So the Doppler shift swings from approximately +280 kHz (approaching) through zero at TCA, to -280 kHz (receding). The ground station receiver must track this shift, typically using a frequency-locked loop.

Figure 3: Doppler shift vs. time through a pass. The frequency offset is positive (blue-shifted) while approaching, zero at TCA, and negative (red-shifted) while receding.

Doppler rate: The rate of change of the Doppler shift (d(delta_f)/dt) is highest at TCA, not at AOS or LOS. This is because the radial velocity changes most rapidly when the satellite passes closest overhead. The ground station's tracking loop must be fast enough to follow this maximum Doppler rate, which can reach ~4 kHz/s for a high-elevation pass.

Range Rate in the Simulator

The simulator computes range rate directly from the ECI velocity vector. In ground-station.ts, the relative velocity between the satellite and the ground station is projected onto the unit vector along the line of sight:

// Range rate = dot(relativeVelocity, unitRangeVector)
const rangeRate = relVel.x * unitR.x + relVel.y * unitR.y + relVel.z * unitR.z;

Note that the ground station itself has velocity because Earth rotates (~0.46 km/s at the equator). The code accounts for this by subtracting the observer's rotational velocity from the satellite's ECI velocity before computing the dot product.

Doppler Compensation in Practice

The Doppler shift must be compensated at the receiver. There are two approaches:

Open-loop pre-compensation: If the satellite's orbit is known (from the TLE), the expected Doppler profile can be predicted in advance. The transmitter or receiver pre-adjusts its local oscillator frequency to cancel the predicted Doppler. This works well for the bulk of the Doppler shift but cannot correct for prediction errors or short-term variations.

Closed-loop tracking: The receiver uses a carrier tracking loop (typically a phase-locked loop or frequency-locked loop) to continuously estimate and track the residual Doppler. The loop has a bandwidth that determines how fast it can respond to Doppler changes. A wider bandwidth tracks faster but admits more noise; a narrower bandwidth is cleaner but may lose lock during rapid Doppler changes.

In practice, both approaches are used together: open-loop compensation removes the predictable Doppler, and the tracking loop handles the residual. The most demanding moment is at TCA, where the Doppler rate is highest (~4 kHz/s for a high-elevation pass). If the tracking loop bandwidth is too narrow, it cannot follow this rapid change and the receiver loses carrier lock -- a condition called "cycle slip" that corrupts data until the loop reacquires.

Doppler and the Link Budget

The Doppler shift affects the link budget indirectly through two mechanisms:

  • Frequency offset: If the Doppler is not perfectly compensated, the remaining offset causes a mismatch between the received signal and the receiver's demodulator, degrading SNR. For typical tracking loop accuracy (~100 Hz residual), this effect is negligible at 12 GHz.
  • Bandwidth spreading: Across the 250 MHz channel bandwidth, different spectral components experience slightly different Doppler shifts (because they have different carrier frequencies). This causes a tiny spectral compression or expansion (~0.002% at maximum Doppler) that is far below any practical concern.

6 Why LEO Passes Are Short

A Starlink satellite at 550 km altitude has an orbital velocity of about 7.6 km/s. Earth's radius is 6371 km. The satellite is visible from a ground station only when it is above the elevation mask -- a relatively small angular window. Let us work through the geometry.

Worked Example: Pass Duration

Step 1: Orbital velocity. From the vis-viva equation for a circular orbit:

v = sqrt(mu / r) = sqrt(398600 / 6921) = 7.59 km/s

Step 2: Angular velocity about Earth's center.

omega = v / r = 7.59 / 6921 = 0.001097 rad/s = 0.0628 deg/s

Step 3: Visible arc. For a satellite at altitude h = 550 km, the half-angle of the visible cone (from Earth's center) at elevation mask el = 25 degrees is:

cos(lambda) = (R_E / (R_E + h)) * cos(el) = (6371 / 6921) * cos(25) = 0.834
lambda = 33.5 degrees

The total visible arc is 2 * lambda = 67 degrees, but the satellite does not necessarily pass through the center of this arc.

Step 4: Maximum pass duration. For an overhead pass (best case):

t_max = 2 * lambda / omega = 67 / 0.0628 = ~1067 seconds = ~17.8 minutes

But this is the theoretical maximum for a directly overhead pass at 0 degrees elevation mask. With the 25-degree mask, the arc shrinks. A typical high-elevation pass (max el = 78 degrees) in the simulator lasts about 480 seconds (8 minutes). Low-elevation passes may last only 2-3 minutes.

Practical constraint: The short pass duration means that every second counts. The antenna must acquire the beam quickly, the frequency tracker must lock to the Doppler-shifted signal, and the adaptive modulation must converge to the optimal coding scheme -- all while the geometry is continuously changing. This is why the simulator models every subsystem at 1-second resolution.

The Earth's Rotation Effect

Earth rotates at about 0.0042 deg/s at the equator. Compared to the satellite's orbital angular velocity of 0.0628 deg/s, Earth's rotation is only about 7% as fast. This means the ground track of the satellite shifts westward by about 22.5 degrees per orbit (1440 minutes / 15.35 revolutions * 0.0042 deg/s * 60 s/min). This is why you do not see the same satellite at the same time every day -- the ground track pattern repeats only after many orbits.

Pass Quality Classification

Not all passes are created equal. The maximum elevation determines how much useful data can be transferred. Engineers classify passes by their peak elevation:

Coordinate Frames

Understanding orbital mechanics requires working in several coordinate frames. The simulator uses three:

ECI (Earth-Centered Inertial): The primary frame for orbital propagation. The origin is at Earth's center. The X-axis points toward the vernal equinox, the Z-axis points toward the north pole, and Y completes the right-handed system. This frame does NOT rotate with the Earth -- it is fixed relative to the stars. SGP4 outputs position and velocity in ECI.

ECEF (Earth-Centered Earth-Fixed): Same origin as ECI, but rotates with the Earth. The X-axis passes through the intersection of the equator and the prime meridian (0 degrees longitude). Converting from ECI to ECEF requires knowing the Greenwich Mean Sidereal Time (GMST), which accounts for Earth's rotation angle at any given moment.

Topocentric (Look Angles): Centered on the ground station. The "up" direction is the local zenith, north and east are along the local horizon. Elevation and azimuth are angles in this frame. The satellite.ecfToLookAngles() function performs the ECEF-to-topocentric conversion.

Why ECI matters: Orbital mechanics equations are simplest in ECI because this frame is approximately inertial (non-rotating, non-accelerating). In ECEF, you would need to add Coriolis and centrifugal pseudo-forces, complicating the equations unnecessarily. The simulator propagates in ECI, then converts to ECEF only when computing look angles from the ground station.

Satellite Footprint and Coverage

The instantaneous coverage area of a satellite at altitude h is the region of Earth's surface from which the satellite appears above a minimum elevation angle. For h = 550 km and an elevation mask of 25 degrees, the footprint has a radius of approximately:

r_footprint = R_E * arccos( R_E * cos(el_min) / (R_E + h) ) - el_min
= 6371 * arccos( 6371 * cos(25) / 6921 ) - 25 deg
= 6371 * arccos(0.8342) - 25 = 6371 * 33.5 - 25 = ~540 km on the ground

This means a single Starlink satellite can serve ground stations within a circle of roughly 540 km radius -- about the size of a large US state. The constellation of 4000+ satellites in the first shell ensures that multiple satellites are usually visible from any point within the coverage latitude band (roughly 53 degrees N to 53 degrees S, matching the orbital inclination).

7 Code Connection

The orbital mechanics subsystem is implemented in three source files under src/simulation/orbit/:

tle.ts -- TLE Parsing and Propagation

This file provides two core functions. parseTLE() takes the raw TLE lines and returns a SatelliteRecord containing the satellite.js satrec object, NORAD ID, and epoch. propagate() wraps satellite.js to produce ECI position and velocity at any requested time.

export function parseTLE(line1: string, line2: string, name = 'STARLINK'): SatelliteRecord {
  const satrec = satellite.twoline2satrec(line1, line2);
  if (satrec.error !== 0) {
    throw new Error(`TLE parse error: code ${satrec.error}`);
  }
  const noradId = line1.substring(2, 7).trim();
  const epoch = parseTleEpoch(line1);
  return { satrec, name, noradId, epoch };
}

The heavy lifting (SGP4 propagation itself) is handled by the satellite.js library. Our code is a thin wrapper that converts the raw TLE text into the internal satrec data structure.

pass-finder.ts -- Finding the Best Pass

The findNextPass() function implements the coarse-scan / fine-refine algorithm described in Section 4. The findBestPass() function searches up to 48 hours to find the highest-elevation pass, then extends the geometry 300 seconds before and after TCA to include the orbital approach and departure phases (for the 3D globe visualization).

// Phase 1: Coarse scan to find when satellite is above elevation mask
for (let t = 0; t < searchDurationMs; t += coarseStepMs) {
  const elev = getElevation(satrec, gs, date);
  if (elev > elevationMask && !wasAboveMask) {
    passStart = t;      // Candidate AOS

}

ground-station.ts -- Look Angles and Range Rate

The computeLookAngles() function takes ECI position and velocity, converts the satellite position to ECEF, then uses satellite.ecfToLookAngles() to get elevation, azimuth, and range. It also computes range rate via the dot product of relative velocity and unit range vector, accounting for Earth's rotation.

// Earth rotation velocity of the observer
const earthRotRate = 7.2921159e-5; // rad/s
const obsVelEci = {
  x: -earthRotRate * observerEci.y,
  y:  earthRotRate * observerEci.x,

// Range rate = dot(relative velocity, unit LOS vector)

9 Self-Assessment Questions

Q1: A Starlink TLE shows mean motion = 15.34524170 rev/day. What is the orbital period in minutes, and what approximate altitude does this correspond to?

Period = 1440 min/day / 15.345 rev/day = 93.8 minutes. Using the relationship between period and semi-major axis (Kepler's third law), T = 2*pi*sqrt(a^3/mu), we get a = 6921 km, which gives an altitude of a - R_E = 6921 - 6371 = ~550 km. This is the standard Starlink v1 orbital shell.

Q2: The TLE's eccentricity is 0.0003259. What is the difference between perigee altitude and apogee altitude?

For a nearly circular orbit with semi-major axis a = 6921 km and eccentricity e = 0.0003259: perigee = a(1-e) - R_E = 6921 * 0.9997 - 6371 = 547.7 km, apogee = a(1+e) - R_E = 6921 * 1.0003 - 6371 = 552.3 km. The difference is only 4.5 km, confirming the orbit is essentially circular. This tiny eccentricity is maintained by periodic station-keeping maneuvers.

Q3: Why does SGP4 use "mean" orbital elements rather than "osculating" (instantaneous) elements? Why would feeding osculating elements into SGP4 give wrong results?

SGP4 is designed as a pair: the TLE generator removes short-period oscillations (caused by J2, etc.) to produce mean elements, and the SGP4 propagator adds those oscillations back analytically. If you fed osculating elements (which already contain the oscillations) into SGP4, it would add the perturbation corrections a second time, producing double-counting errors in the predicted position. This is why you cannot mix TLE data with a different propagator (like a numerical integrator) without first converting the element type. The TLE format is inseparable from the SGP4 algorithm.

Q4: A satellite pass has maximum elevation of 45 degrees. Estimate the slant range at TCA and at AOS (elevation = 25 degrees). How much does the free-space path loss differ between these two moments?

Using d = R_E [sqrt((h/R_E + 1)^2 - cos^2(el)) - sin(el)] with R_E = 6371, h = 550: At TCA (el=45): d = 6371 * [sqrt(1.0864^2 - cos^2(45)) - sin(45)] = 6371 * [sqrt(1.180 - 0.500) - 0.707] = 6371 * [0.824 - 0.707] = ~750 km. At AOS (el=25): d = ~1100 km. The path loss difference = 20*log10(1100/750) = 20*log10(1.467) = ~3.3 dB. This means the link at AOS has 3.3 dB less margin than at TCA, just from geometry alone -- before accounting for atmospheric losses and scan angle effects.

Q5: At what point during a pass is the Doppler shift zero, and at what point is the Doppler RATE (d/dt of frequency) highest? Why?

The Doppler shift is zero at TCA (time of closest approach), because at that moment the satellite's velocity is perpendicular to the line of sight, so the radial velocity component is zero. The Doppler rate (rate of change of the Doppler shift) is highest at TCA, not at AOS or LOS. This is because the radial velocity swings through zero most rapidly when the geometry changes fastest -- which happens when the satellite is closest. At AOS and LOS, the satellite is far away and the angle changes slowly, so the Doppler rate is actually lower. This maximum Doppler rate at TCA is the hardest moment for the ground station's frequency tracking loop.

Q6: The simulator's pass-finder uses a coarse scan at 30-second intervals. Could this miss a valid pass? Under what conditions?

Yes, it could miss a very short, low-elevation pass. If the satellite barely rises above the 25-degree mask for less than 30 seconds, the coarse scan might step over the entire above-mask window without detecting it. This would happen for passes where the maximum elevation is only slightly above 25 degrees (say, 26-28 degrees). However, these passes are not useful for communications anyway because the link quality would be marginal. The findBestPass() function also rejects passes shorter than 10 seconds and with maximum elevation below 15 degrees, so the practical impact is negligible. A more robust approach would be to use a variable step size that decreases near the horizon.
