Unit 6

Satellite Power Systems

Power is the lifeblood of a spacecraft. Every subsystem -- from the phased array antenna to the onboard processor -- draws from a finite electrical budget. In LEO, that budget swings dramatically between sunlit and eclipse portions of each 90-minute orbit. This unit covers how a satellite generates, stores, and distributes power, and how power constraints directly limit communication throughput.

Learning Objectives

  • Calculate solar panel output as a function of cell efficiency, panel area, solar flux, and sun angle
  • Explain why a LEO satellite experiences ~15 eclipse cycles per day and size the battery accordingly
  • Model battery state-of-charge dynamics including charge, discharge, and capacity fade
  • Draw the power bus architecture from solar array to load
  • Enumerate spacecraft loads and explain how total power demand is distributed
  • Describe the four power modes and how they constrain the transmitter
  • Trace how power limitations propagate through to PA output, SNR, and throughput

1. Solar Power Generation

LEO satellites generate electrical power from sunlight using photovoltaic solar panels. The dominant technology is the gallium arsenide (GaAs) triple-junction cell, which stacks three semiconductor layers optimized for different portions of the solar spectrum.

Triple-Junction Cell Physics

Each junction absorbs a different wavelength band:

  • Top junction (GaInP): Absorbs high-energy UV/blue photons (~1.9 eV bandgap)
  • Middle junction (GaAs): Absorbs visible-spectrum photons (~1.4 eV bandgap)
  • Bottom junction (Ge): Absorbs low-energy infrared photons (~0.67 eV bandgap)

By capturing photons at three different energy levels, the cell extracts significantly more energy than a single-junction silicon cell. Typical beginning-of-life (BOL) efficiency is ~30%, compared to ~20% for terrestrial silicon panels.

Power Output Formula

Psolar = η × A × S × cos(θsun)

Where:

  • η = cell efficiency (~0.30 for GaAs triple-junction)
  • A = total panel area (m²) -- typically 10-15 m² for a LEO comms satellite
  • S = solar irradiance at 1 AU = 1361 W/m² (the "solar constant")
  • θsun = angle between the panel normal and the sun vector

Worked Example

A satellite has 12 m² of GaAs panels at 30% efficiency, with the sun at 20 degrees off-normal:

P_solar = 0.30 * 12 * 1361 * cos(20deg)
        = 0.30 * 12 * 1361 * 0.9397
        = 4598 * 0.9397
        = 4321 W

At θ=0 (perfect alignment): P = 4898 W. At θ=60: P = 2449 W. The cosine factor matters enormously -- a 60-degree misalignment halves the power output.

Why cos(θ) Matters

The cosine term accounts for the effective cross-sectional area of the panel as seen from the sun direction. When the panel tilts away from the sun, less solar flux is intercepted per unit of panel area. Think of holding a piece of paper flat toward a light vs. tilting it: the shadow it casts shrinks.

Degradation Over Lifetime

Solar cells degrade in the space environment due to radiation damage (proton and electron bombardment in the Van Allen belts), thermal cycling stress, and micrometeorite impacts. Typical degradation is 2-3% per year. After 5 years, a panel at 30% BOL efficiency may deliver only ~26% effective efficiency -- a ~13% power reduction.

2. Eclipse Cycles

A LEO satellite at 550 km altitude completes one orbit every ~95 minutes. During each orbit, the satellite passes through Earth's shadow for approximately 35 minutes. Since the satellite completes ~15 orbits per day, it experiences ~15 eclipse transitions per day.

Why Eclipse Duration Varies

The shadow fraction depends on orbital altitude and the angle between the orbital plane and the sun vector (the "beta angle", β). When β is near zero, the orbit cuts directly through the widest part of Earth's shadow. When |β| exceeds a critical angle (~71 degrees for 550 km), the satellite never enters eclipse at all -- it is in permanent sunlight.

Eclipse fraction ≈ (1/π) × arccos(√(h² + 2Rh) / ((R + h) × cos(β)))

Where R = Earth radius (6371 km) and h = orbital altitude (550 km). For β=0 at 550 km, the eclipse fraction is about 37% of each orbit, which gives roughly 35 minutes of darkness per 95-minute orbit.

The Power Challenge

During eclipse, solar power drops to zero. All spacecraft loads must be sustained entirely by the battery for the full 35-minute shadow period. This is the fundamental sizing constraint for the battery: it must store enough energy to power all essential loads through the longest expected eclipse.

Eclipse Energy Budget

If total spacecraft load is 200 W and eclipse lasts 35 minutes:

E_eclipse = 200 W * 35 min * (1 hr / 60 min) = 116.7 Wh

With 80% depth-of-discharge limit:
E_battery_min = 116.7 / 0.80 = 145.8 Wh

Actual battery: 2500 Wh (provides large margin for
aging, load spikes, and reduced power modes)

3. Battery System

The onboard battery is a lithium-ion (Li-ion) pack with a capacity of approximately 2500 Wh. Li-ion is the standard chemistry for modern LEO spacecraft because of its high specific energy (~150-200 Wh/kg), long cycle life, and well-understood behavior.

State of Charge Dynamics

The battery's State of Charge (SoC) changes each simulation tick based on the net power flow:

ΔSoC = (Pnet × Δt) / Ecapacity

Where:

  • Pnet = Psolar - Pload (positive when charging, negative when discharging)
  • Δt = time step (1 second in the simulator, expressed in hours for Wh units)
  • Ecapacity = battery capacity in Wh (2500 Wh)

SoC Example

During sunlight with full solar power:

P_solar = 4500 W, P_load = 200 W
P_net = 4500 - 200 = +4300 W (charging)
dSoC per second = 4300 * (1/3600) / 2500 = 0.000478
                = 0.048% per second
                = 2.87% per minute

During eclipse:
P_solar = 0 W, P_load = 200 W
P_net = -200 W (discharging)
dSoC per second = -200 * (1/3600) / 2500 = -0.0000222
                = -0.00222% per second
                = -0.133% per minute
                = -4.67% over 35-minute eclipse

Discharge Rate Limits

Li-ion batteries have maximum safe discharge rates, typically expressed as a C-rate. A 2500 Wh battery at 1C can deliver 2500 W continuously. At the typical 200 W load, the satellite operates at only ~0.08C -- well within safe limits. However, transient peak loads (e.g., attitude control thruster firing) can spike demand briefly, and the power management unit must ensure C-rate limits are never exceeded.

Capacity Fade and Aging

Every charge-discharge cycle degrades the battery slightly. With ~15 cycles per day, a LEO satellite battery experiences ~5500 cycles per year. Over a 5-year mission lifetime, this totals ~27,500 cycles. Li-ion capacity fade follows an approximately square-root-of-cycles relationship:

Eactual(n) = EBOL × (1 - k × √n)

Where k is a degradation constant (~0.0005 for space-grade cells) and n is cycle count. After 27,500 cycles: capacity retention ~96%, or ~2400 Wh. Mission planners design for end-of-life (EOL) capacity, ensuring the battery can still sustain eclipse loads even after years of cycling.

4. Power Bus Architecture

The power bus is the central electrical backbone of the spacecraft. It regulates voltage, routes power from sources to loads, and manages transitions between solar and battery power. The architecture follows a regulated bus topology.

Power Conditioning

The Maximum Power Point Tracker (MPPT) extracts optimal power from the solar array by continuously adjusting the operating voltage. Solar cells have a characteristic I-V curve with a single maximum power point that shifts with temperature and illumination. The MPPT locks onto this point and feeds regulated DC to the bus.

The bus voltage is typically regulated at 28V (a common spacecraft standard). Each load subsystem has its own DC-DC converter to step down (or up) to its required voltage. This architecture isolates load transients from the bus and prevents one failing subsystem from bringing down others.

A critical insight: the PA consumes only 3-7.5% of total power. Yet it is the first subsystem to be throttled in low-power modes because reducing PA power has the most direct impact on the link budget. Every watt removed from the PA reduces EIRP, degrades SNR, and forces modulation downshift.

Why is digital processing the largest load? The onboard processor handles packet routing for thousands of simultaneous users, beamforming calculations for the phased array, and inter-satellite link management. Modern LEO communication satellites are essentially flying routers with antennas attached.

6. Power Modes

The power management system defines four operating modes based on battery State of Charge. As SoC drops, loads are progressively shed to protect the battery from deep discharge, which accelerates aging and risks permanent damage.

ModeSoC RangeNameActions
0> 40%NominalAll subsystems at full power. PA allowed 6-15W.
130 - 40%ReducedISL power halved, compute reduced 30%. PA unchanged.
220 - 30%Low PowerPA power capped at 8W. Non-essential processing off.
3< 20%SurvivalPA OFF. Only ADCS + housekeeping + thermal active.

State Machine

Hysteresis Logic

The 5% hysteresis gap is critical. Without it, a satellite hovering at exactly 40% SoC would oscillate rapidly between Mode 0 and Mode 1 every tick, causing load flapping, thermal transients, and modulation instability. The gap ensures that once a lower power mode is entered, the battery must recover significantly before the system returns to higher power.

7. How Power Constrains the PA

This is where power management directly impacts communication performance. The power management unit sets a paAllowedPower_W limit based on the current power mode. The PA must operate within this budget.

The Cascade

  1. Power mode limits PA input power. In Mode 2, the PA is capped at 8W instead of the nominal 15W maximum.
  2. Less PA power means more backoff. If the PA was operating at 12W and the budget drops to 8W, the amplifier must increase its output backoff (OBO) to reduce output.
  3. More backoff reduces EIRP. The satellite's effective radiated power drops by the amount of additional backoff applied.
  4. Lower EIRP degrades SNR. The link budget equation directly includes EIRP. Losing 2 dB of PA power means losing 2 dB of SNR at the receiver.
  5. Lower SNR forces modulation downshift. If SNR drops below the threshold for 16QAM, the adaptive modulation system shifts to QPSK, cutting spectral efficiency in half.
  6. Lower spectral efficiency reduces throughput. The data rate drops proportionally.

Numeric Example: Power Mode Cascade

Mode 0 (nominal):
  PA power: 12W  ->  EIRP: 47 dBW  ->  SNR: 18 dB
  ModCod: 16QAM 3/4 (requires 13.5 dB)  ->  Throughput: 750 Mbps

Mode 2 (SoC=25%):
  PA power: 8W   ->  EIRP: 45.2 dBW  ->  SNR: 16.2 dB
  ModCod: 16QAM 2/3 (requires 12 dB)   ->  Throughput: 667 Mbps
  Loss: ~11%

Mode 3 (SoC=18%):
  PA power: 0W   ->  No transmission  ->  Throughput: 0 Mbps
  LINK DOWN -- satellite in survival mode

This cascade is a central theme of the simulator: power is not just a spacecraft subsystem -- it is a throughput-limiting constraint. A satellite entering eclipse with a partially depleted battery may be forced into Mode 1 or Mode 2 mid-pass, causing a visible throughput drop that has nothing to do with orbital geometry or atmospheric conditions.

8. Power Dynamics During a Pass

To see all the pieces working together, consider a typical pass scenario:

  1. Pre-pass (sunlit): Battery at 95% SoC. Solar panels producing 4500 W. Battery charging at ~4300 W. Mode 0.
  2. Pass begins (eclipse entry): Satellite enters Earth's shadow 2 minutes before the ground station pass. Solar power drops to zero. Battery begins discharging at 200 W.
  3. Mid-pass (eclipse): Battery at 92% SoC. Still well within Mode 0. PA runs at full power. Throughput limited only by geometry and atmospheric conditions.
  4. Post-pass (still in eclipse): Pass ends after 8 minutes. Battery at ~91% SoC. Satellite continues in eclipse for another 25 minutes. SoC drops to ~86%.
  5. Eclipse exit: Solar panels illuminate. Charging resumes. SoC recovery begins.

This is the healthy scenario. Now consider a degraded one: the battery starts the pass at 42% SoC (perhaps after extended power anomaly or multiple consecutive eclipses with reduced solar input). During the pass, SoC drops below 40% and the satellite enters Mode 1. ISL and compute are reduced. If the pass is long enough and load stays high, SoC could approach 30%, triggering Mode 2 and PA capping -- mid-pass. The ground user would see throughput degrade over the course of the pass for reasons entirely internal to the spacecraft.

9. Code Connection

The power system simulation lives in:

  • src/simulation/power/power-bus.ts -- The main power simulation module. Contains two key functions:
    • createInitialPowerState() -- Initializes the power state with default SoC, panel efficiency, battery capacity, and mode thresholds.
    • stepPower(state, dt, inSunlight, sunAngle) -- Called each simulation tick. Computes solar power generation, net power flow, SoC update, mode transitions (with hysteresis), and the resulting PA power limit.

Key logic in stepPower:

// 1. Compute solar power
const solarPower = inSunlight
  ? efficiency * area * SOLAR_CONSTANT * Math.cos(sunAngle)
  : 0;

// 2. Compute net power
const netPower = solarPower - totalLoad;

// 3. Update SoC
state.soc += (netPower * dt_hours) / state.batteryCapacity;
state.soc = Math.max(0, Math.min(1, state.soc));

// 4. Determine power mode (with hysteresis)
if (state.soc < 0.20) state.mode = 3;
else if (state.soc < 0.30 && state.mode >= 2) state.mode = 2;
else if (state.soc < 0.40 && state.mode >= 1) state.mode = 1;
// Recovery thresholds are 5% higher
else if (state.soc > 0.45 && state.mode === 1) state.mode = 0;
else if (state.soc > 0.35 && state.mode === 2) state.mode = 1;
else if (state.soc > 0.25 && state.mode === 3) state.mode = 2;

// 5. Set PA power limit
const paLimits = [15, 15, 8, 0]; // Watts per mode
state.paAllowedPower = paLimits[state.mode];

The output paAllowedPower feeds directly into the RF chain model, which adjusts PA backoff and recomputes EIRP. From there, the link budget, modulation selection, and packet simulation all see the downstream effects.

At what SoC does it recover back to Mode 1? Back to Mode 0? Draw the SoC-vs-time trajectory and label each mode transition.
