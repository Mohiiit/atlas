Unit 02

Phased Array Antennas

How electronic beam steering works, why it degrades at high scan angles, and how the simulator models every gain component from element pattern to pointing loss.

Learning Objectives

  • Explain why phased arrays are used instead of mechanically steered dishes for LEO satellite communications
  • Describe the cos^q(theta) element radiation pattern and its physical origin
  • Derive how inter-element phase shifts create constructive interference in a desired direction
  • Calculate the array factor gain and explain the role of taper efficiency
  • Identify the three physical mechanisms behind scan loss and predict gain degradation at any scan angle
  • Predict the impact of element failures on array gain and sidelobe levels

1 Why Phased Arrays?

A LEO satellite passes over a ground station in about 8 minutes, during which the beam must track continuously from one horizon to the other. The antenna needs to slew across more than 100 degrees of sky in minutes. A traditional parabolic dish with a mechanical gimbal can do this, but it has critical disadvantages for spacecraft:

PropertyMechanical DishPhased Array
Steering speedLimited by motor torque; typically ~10 deg/sMicroseconds (electronic phase shift)
Moving partsGimbals, bearings, motors -- all failure pointsNone; solid state
Form factorCurved reflector; hard to stow for launchFlat panel; fits in launch stack
Multi-beamOne beam per reflectorCan form multiple beams simultaneously
VibrationMoving mass causes reaction torques on spacecraftNo moving mass; no torque
LifetimeMechanical wear limits life to ~5-7 yearsNo wear-out mechanism; 10+ years

The fundamental trade-off is that phased arrays sacrifice peak gain at extreme scan angles for the enormous advantage of having no moving parts. For a LEO constellation that launches thousands of satellites, the reliability and manufacturability of a flat-panel phased array vastly outweighs the gain penalty at the edge of scan.

Starlink array specs: The user terminal (ground station) and the satellite both use phased arrays. The satellite array has approximately 1200 elements operating at Ku-band (12 GHz), with element spacing of lambda/2 = 12.5 mm. Maximum usable scan angle is approximately 70 degrees off boresight.

2 The Single Antenna Element

Each element in the phased array is a microstrip patch antenna -- a flat conducting rectangle on a dielectric substrate, backed by a ground plane. It is cheap to manufacture, low-profile (a few millimeters thick), and naturally radiates a broadside beam (perpendicular to the patch surface).

The cos^q(theta) Pattern

The radiation pattern of a single patch element is well approximated by a power-of-cosine envelope:

G_element(theta) = G_max * cos^q(theta)
In dB: G_element_dBi(theta) = G_max_dBi + q * 10 * log10(cos(theta))

Where theta is the angle off boresight (0 = perpendicular to the array face), G_max is the peak gain at boresight, and q is the pattern exponent that controls the beamwidth.

Physical origin: The cos(theta) factor arises from the projected aperture area of the patch shrinking as you look at it from an angle. An observer at angle theta sees the patch foreshortened by cos(theta), and since gain is proportional to effective area, the gain drops accordingly. The exponent q > 1 accounts for additional rolloff from the finite ground plane, surface wave excitation, and substrate effects. For a Ku-band patch with moderate substrate permittivity (epsilon_r ~ 3), q = 1.35 is a typical measured value.

In the simulator, the element peak gain is 5.0 dBi and q = 1.35. Here are concrete gain values at several angles:

3 How Beam Steering Works

The fundamental insight of phased arrays: by introducing a progressive phase shift across the elements, the wavefronts from all elements arrive in-phase at a desired angle, not just at boresight. The beam appears to "steer" without any physical motion.

Phase Shift Principle

Consider a linear array of N elements spaced d apart. To steer the beam to angle theta_s from boresight, element n (counting from 0) must have an additional phase shift:

phi_n = n * k * d * sin(theta_s)

where k = 2*pi/lambda is the free-space wavenumber. This progressive phase compensates for the extra path length that each successive element's signal must travel to arrive at angle theta_s. When all the wavefronts add up in-phase at theta_s, they constructively interfere and produce a strong beam.

For example, with lambda/2 spacing at 12 GHz and a desired scan to 30 degrees: phi = k * (lambda/2) * sin(30) = (2*pi/lambda) * (lambda/2) * 0.5 = pi/2 = 90 degrees per element. Each successive element gets an additional 90-degree phase shift.

Figure 2: Beam steering by progressive phase shift. Left: all elements in-phase produce a boresight beam. Right: 90-degree phase steps steer the beam 30 degrees off boresight. The wavefronts tilt to match the new beam direction.

Why lambda/2 Spacing?

The element spacing d = lambda/2 = 12.5 mm at 12 GHz is not arbitrary. It is the maximum spacing that avoids grating lobes -- spurious beams that appear when the inter-element path difference equals a full wavelength. With d = lambda/2:

Grating lobe condition: sin(theta_grating) = sin(theta_scan) + lambda/d
For d = lambda/2: sin(theta_grating) = sin(theta_scan) + 2
Since |sin| <= 1, no grating lobe exists in real space.

If the spacing were larger -- say d = lambda (25 mm) -- then scanning to just 30 degrees would produce a grating lobe at sin(theta_g) = 0.5 + 1 = 1.5... still invisible. But at d = 0.8*lambda, a scan to 40 degrees produces sin(theta_g) = sin(40) + 1/0.8 = 0.643 + 1.25 = 1.89... still fine. The problem begins with spacings above lambda, where grating lobes enter the visible hemisphere during large scans. The lambda/2 rule provides a universal safety guarantee for all scan angles up to 90 degrees.

Phase Shifter Resolution

In practice, the phase shift at each element is applied by a digital phase shifter -- a component that can set the phase to one of 2^b discrete values, where b is the number of bits. Common values are 5-bit (32 states, 11.25 degree resolution) and 6-bit (64 states, 5.625 degree resolution).

The quantization error from finite phase shifter resolution has two effects: (1) a slight reduction in directivity (because the beam is not steered to exactly the desired angle), and (2) increased sidelobes from the periodic quantization error. For a 6-bit phase shifter, the quantization loss is about 0.2 dB and the sidelobe degradation is negligible. For a 5-bit shifter, the loss increases to about 0.5 dB.

The simulator does not model phase shifter quantization directly (it uses continuous phase shifts), but the pointing jitter model partially captures this effect through the quantization contribution to pointing error.

Frequency Dependence

The phase shift required for a given scan angle depends on frequency through the wavenumber k = 2*pi*f/c. If the array is designed to steer to angle theta_s at the center frequency f_0, then at a different frequency f within the bandwidth, the actual beam direction shifts to:

sin(theta_actual) = sin(theta_s) * f_0 / f

This effect is called beam squint. For a 250 MHz bandwidth centered at 12 GHz, the fractional frequency range is +/- 1.04%, which causes a beam squint of about 0.6 degrees at 60-degree scan. This is smaller than the beamwidth at that scan angle (5 degrees), so it does not cause significant loss for narrowband signals. However, for very wideband signals, true-time-delay steering (instead of phase shifting) would be needed to avoid squint.

4 Array Factor and Total Gain

When N identical elements are coherently combined (all signals added in-phase), the directivity increases by a factor of N relative to a single element. This is the array factor. The total gain is:

G_array = 10*log10(N) + G_element(theta) + 10*log10(eta_taper)

For the simulator's 1200-element array at boresight:

ComponentValueExplanation
Array factor10*log10(1200) = 30.8 dBCoherent combining of 1200 elements
Element gain (boresight)5.0 dBiSingle patch element at theta = 0
Taper efficiency10*log10(0.85) = -0.7 dBLoss from Taylor amplitude taper
Total at boresight35.1 dBiBefore scan loss or pointing loss

Taper Efficiency: The Sidelobe Trade-off

A uniform amplitude distribution (all elements at equal power) maximizes directivity but produces the highest sidelobes -- about -13.3 dB below the main beam for a linear array. High sidelobes are unacceptable for satellite communications because they cause interference with adjacent beams and neighboring satellites.

To suppress sidelobes, the array uses amplitude tapering: center elements radiate at full power while edge elements are attenuated, following a Taylor window distribution. The Taylor taper achieves configurable sidelobe levels (typically -25 to -30 dB) at the cost of modest directivity reduction. This reduction is captured by the taper efficiency eta_taper.

In the simulator, eta_taper = 0.85, meaning the tapered 1200-element array has the directivity of an approximately 1020-element uniform array. The 0.7 dB cost buys a 12 dB improvement in sidelobe suppression -- a worthwhile trade for a multi-beam satellite system.

Why taper matters for Starlink: Each Starlink satellite serves multiple ground stations simultaneously using spatially separated beams. If sidelobes from one beam leak into an adjacent beam's coverage area, users in that area see interference. The Taylor taper ensures that the sidelobe power landing on neighboring coverage cells is low enough to maintain usable signal-to-interference ratios.

5 Scan Loss -- The Critical Concept

Scan loss is the single most important degradation mechanism in a phased array. As the beam steers away from boresight, the effective gain drops. This is not one effect but three distinct physical mechanisms acting simultaneously.

Mechanism 1: Projected Aperture Reduction

A flat panel viewed from angle theta has an apparent area of A * cos(theta). Since antenna gain is proportional to effective aperture, this gives a direct penalty:

L_aperture = 10 * log10( cos(theta) ) dB

At 60 degrees: cos(60) = 0.5, so the aperture loss is exactly -3.0 dB. The physical area has not changed, but the electromagnetic "shadow" the array casts in the beam direction has halved.

Figure 3: Projected aperture at three scan angles. At 60 degrees, the array "looks" half as wide to an observer in the beam direction.

Mechanism 2: Active Reflection Coefficient

Each element in a phased array does not radiate in isolation. Electromagnetic coupling between adjacent elements means that each element's effective impedance depends on the excitation of its neighbors. When the beam is steered, the progressive phase shift changes the coupling pattern, shifting the effective impedance away from the matched condition.

This is the active impedance (or scan impedance): the impedance each element "sees" when all elements are radiating together. At boresight, the array is designed to match 50 ohms. As the beam scans, the active impedance drifts, causing an increasing fraction of power to be reflected back into the feed network:

Mismatch efficiency = 1 - |Gamma(theta)|^2

where |Gamma| is the magnitude of the active reflection coefficient. In the simulator, |Gamma| rises from about 0.05 at boresight to 0.25 at 60 degrees, costing approximately 0.3 dB in mismatch loss. This seems small, but at extreme scan (70+ degrees), the mismatch can reach 0.8+ dB.

The simulator computes this using a nearest-neighbor mutual coupling model: the active impedance is the self-impedance plus the sum of nearest-neighbor mutual impedances weighted by the scan-dependent phase factors:

// Active impedance from coupling model
const psiX = k * d * Math.sin(theta) * Math.cos(scanAzimuth);
const psiY = k * d * Math.sin(theta) * Math.sin(scanAzimuth);
const couplingFactor = 2 * Math.cos(psiX) + 2 * Math.cos(psiY);

Mechanism 3: Mutual Coupling Variation

Beyond impedance mismatch, mutual coupling also modifies each element's radiation pattern at scan. Edge elements see different coupling than center elements. This non-uniformity degrades coherent combining efficiency and raises sidelobe levels. The effect is most pronounced at large scan angles where the phase gradient across the array is steepest.

Combined Scan Loss

The simulator combines projected aperture and mismatch into a single scan efficiency:

eta_scan(theta) = cos(theta) * (1 - |Gamma(theta)|^2)
Scan Loss (dB) = 10 * log10( eta_scan )

The double penalty at low elevation: When a satellite is near the horizon (low elevation), two effects compound: (1) the slant range is large, increasing free-space path loss by 6+ dB relative to zenith, and (2) the phased array must steer to a large scan angle, adding 3-5 dB of scan loss. At the 25-degree elevation mask, the total geometric disadvantage relative to an overhead pass can exceed 10 dB -- a factor of 10 in received power. This is why maximum elevation is the single most important predictor of pass quality.

6 Beamwidth Widening

The 3 dB beamwidth at boresight is determined by the aperture size. For the simulator's ~1200-element array at Ku-band with lambda/2 spacing, the boresight beamwidth is approximately 2.5 degrees.

As the beam steers, the projected aperture dimension along the scan plane shrinks by cos(theta). Since beamwidth is inversely proportional to aperture dimension:

bw(theta) = bw_0 / cos(theta)
Scan AngleBeamwidthRelative Broadening
0 deg2.5 deg1.0x (reference)
30 deg2.9 deg1.15x
45 deg3.5 deg1.41x
60 deg5.0 deg2.0x
70 deg7.3 deg2.92x

A wider beam has two consequences: (1) the directivity is lower (same power spread over a larger solid angle), which is already captured in the scan loss, and (2) the beam is actually less sensitive to small pointing errors, because a given angular jitter represents a smaller fraction of the beamwidth.

Why Beamwidth Matters for System Design

The boresight beamwidth of 2.5 degrees has direct system-level implications. Consider the angular size of the ground coverage cell: from 550 km altitude, a 2.5-degree beam illuminates a spot roughly 24 km across on the ground. This means the satellite can serve individual ground cells of about this size, and must form separate beams for cells further apart.

At 60-degree scan, the broadened 5.0-degree beam illuminates a larger cell (~43 km) at greater range. This broader beam means less spatial reuse -- adjacent cells are more likely to receive interference from the widened main beam. The beam broadening at scan is one reason why cell capacity drops at the edge of the satellite's coverage footprint.

Beamwidth and Gain: The Fundamental Link

There is an approximate relationship between beamwidth and directivity for a pencil beam antenna:

D = 4 * pi / (bw_az * bw_el) [in radians]
D_dBi = 10 * log10( 4 * pi / (bw_az_rad * bw_el_rad) )

For a circular beam with bw = 2.5 degrees (0.0436 rad): D = 4*pi / (0.0436)^2 = 6612 = 38.2 dBi. This overestimates our actual 35.1 dBi because the formula assumes 100% aperture efficiency, while our array has taper losses and is not perfectly circular. But the order of magnitude is correct and shows why narrow beamwidth correlates with high gain.

Pointing Loss

Real satellite pointing systems have small jitter from star tracker noise, reaction wheel vibration, and phase-shifter quantization. The simulator models this as a Rayleigh-distributed error with sigma = 0.1 degrees RMS. The resulting pointing loss follows the standard Gaussian beam approximation:

L_pointing = 12 * (delta / bw)^2 [dB]

This formula gives exactly 3 dB loss when the pointing error equals half the beamwidth (at the half-power point), which is correct by definition. Under normal conditions (0.1 degree jitter, 2.5 degree beamwidth), pointing loss is negligible (~0.02 dB). It becomes significant only when jitter increases due to attitude control faults or when the fault model injects extra pointing error.

7 Element Failures and Graceful Degradation

One of the great advantages of phased arrays: graceful degradation. If a single transmit/receive module fails, the array loses only 1/N of its aperture. The beam still forms; the gain drops slightly and sidelobes rise slightly. There is no single point of failure.

Gain Loss from Failed Elements

If the array has N total elements and M have failed, the gain change is:

Delta G = 10 * log10( (N - M) / N ) dB
Failed Elements% FailedGain LossImpact Assessment
0 / 12000%0.00 dBNominal
12 / 12001%-0.04 dBNegligible; within measurement uncertainty
60 / 12005%-0.22 dBWell within link budget margin
120 / 120010%-0.46 dBDetectable but manageable; the "10% rule"
240 / 120020%-0.97 dBApproaching 1 dB; marginal passes may fail
600 / 120050%-3.01 dBHalf the elements; link fails at low elevation
The 10% rule of thumb: Losing 10% of elements costs about 0.46 dB in gain. This is typically within the link budget's design margin. A satellite can operate through its entire design life with 10% element failures without falling below minimum performance requirements. This is why phased arrays are said to "degrade gracefully."

Sidelobe Impact of Element Failures

Failed elements create "holes" in the aperture illumination function. If the failures are randomly distributed, the effect is equivalent to adding a noise floor to the taper pattern. The peak random sidelobe level rises as:

SLL_random = 10 * log10( 1 / (N - M) ) relative to peak

For 1200 elements with 120 failed (10%): the random failure sidelobe floor is -30.3 dB below the main beam. Since the designed Taylor taper sidelobes are about -25 dB, the failure-induced floor is still below the designed level. At 10% failure, the sidelobe structure is essentially unchanged. At 20-30% failure, the random floor begins to dominate and sidelobes rise noticeably, potentially causing interference with adjacent beams.

Coherence Loss

The simulator also models a subtler effect: when subarrays have varying health levels (partially degraded rather than fully failed), the amplitude distribution across the aperture becomes non-uniform in an uncontrolled way. This reduces the coherent combining efficiency beyond the simple N-M model. The coherenceLoss_dB() function in the code computes this by comparing the actual amplitude-weighted sum to the ideal case.

Failure Patterns: Random vs. Clustered

The impact of element failures depends on their spatial distribution. Random failures (e.g., from manufacturing defects or gradual wear-out) are the best case -- they produce the lowest sidelobe degradation because the "holes" in the aperture are evenly spread. Clustered failures (e.g., a power supply failure affecting a subarray sector) are worse: they create a large contiguous gap in the aperture, producing a single strong grating-like sidelobe.

The simulator models this through the subarrayHealth array and the failedSectorFraction parameter. A sector failure (setting a fraction of adjacent elements to zero) causes more coherence loss than the same number of randomly distributed failures.

8 Code Connection

The antenna subsystem is implemented in four source files under src/simulation/antenna/:

element-pattern.ts -- Individual Element Gain

Implements the cos^q(theta) model. Returns element gain in dBi, clamped to -50 dBi for angles beyond 90 degrees.

export function elementGain_dBi(theta_deg: number): number {
  if (absTheta >= 90) return GAIN_FLOOR_DBI;  // -50 dBi
  const gain_dBi =
    ELEMENT_MAX_GAIN_DBI +                       // 5.0 dBi
    ELEMENT_PATTERN_EXPONENT * 10 * Math.log10(cosTheta); // q=1.35
  return Math.max(gain_dBi, GAIN_FLOOR_DBI);
}

scan-loss.ts -- Physics-Based Scan Model

Combines projected aperture and active impedance mismatch. The activeImpedance() function computes scan-dependent impedance from a nearest-neighbor coupling model. The scanLoss_dB() function returns the combined scan loss.

export function scanLoss_dB(theta_deg: number): number {
  const projectedAperture = Math.cos(theta_rad);
  const gamma = activeReflectionCoefficient(clampedTheta);
  const mismatchFactor = 1 - gamma * gamma;
  return 10 * Math.log10(projectedAperture * mismatchFactor);
}

effective-gain.ts -- Full Gain Chain

Assembles all components into a final effective gain figure. Includes the array factor, element gain, taper efficiency, scan loss, beamwidth computation, deterministic pointing jitter (Mulberry32 PRNG for reproducibility), pointing loss, beam shape loss, and coherence loss from subarray health.

const arrGain = arrayFactor_dB + elGain + taperLoss_dB;
const scan = scanLoss_dB(steeringAngle_deg);
const bw = beamwidth_deg(steeringAngle_deg);
const ptLoss = pointingLoss_dB(ptError, bw);
const effective = arrGain + scan - ptLoss - beamShapeLoss - coherenceLoss;

index.ts -- Per-Tick Entry Point

The computeAntennaState() function is called once per simulation tick. It clamps steering angle to the maximum (70 degrees), clamps active elements, and returns the complete AntennaState including beam quality percentage and a steering-limited flag that warns when the satellite is being asked to steer beyond its physical capability.

The Full Signal Chain for One Element

To put the antenna model in context, here is the signal chain for a single transmit element in the phased array:

  1. Digital beamformer: Computes the required amplitude and phase for this element based on the desired beam direction and taper window. Outputs a digital I/Q sample.
  2. DAC: Converts the digital sample to an analog baseband signal.
  3. Upconverter: Mixes the baseband signal with the local oscillator to produce the 12 GHz RF signal.
  4. Phase shifter: Applies the scan-dependent phase shift (the progressive delay that steers the beam).
  5. Variable attenuator: Sets the amplitude taper for this element's position in the Taylor window.
  6. Power amplifier (T/R module): Amplifies the signal to the required transmit power level.
  7. Patch antenna: Radiates the amplified signal into free space with the cos^q(theta) element pattern.

Each element has its own T/R (transmit/receive) module containing steps 3-6. This is what makes phased arrays expensive and complex: for 1200 elements, you need 1200 T/R modules, each containing an upconverter, phase shifter, attenuator, and PA. The element failure model in the simulator corresponds to a T/R module failure -- when a module fails, that element contributes zero signal to the array.

Receive Mode

The phased array also operates in receive mode (the ground terminal receiving the satellite downlink). In receive mode, the signal chain is reversed: each element receives a signal through its patch antenna, amplifies it with a low-noise amplifier (LNA), applies the scan-dependent phase shift, and feeds the result to the digital beamformer, which coherently sums all element signals.

The receiver gain computation is nearly identical to the transmitter, with the same scan loss and beamwidth formulas. The main difference is that the receiver noise temperature depends on the scan angle: at low elevation angles, the element pattern points more toward the warm Earth (255 K noise temperature) rather than cold sky, increasing the system noise temperature and degrading G/T.

10 Self-Assessment Questions

Q1: A phased array has 1200 elements, each with 5 dBi peak gain, and a taper efficiency of 0.85. What is the total boresight gain? Show your calculation step by step.

G_array = 10*log10(N) + G_element + 10*log10(eta_taper) = 10*log10(1200) + 5.0 + 10*log10(0.85) = 30.79 + 5.0 + (-0.71) = 35.08 dBi. The array factor (30.8 dB) dominates. The taper efficiency costs only 0.71 dB -- a small price for sidelobe levels reduced from -13.3 dB to about -25 dB.

Q2: Why can't you simply increase the number of elements to compensate for scan loss at high angles? What are the fundamental and practical limits?

Adding more elements increases boresight gain via 10*log10(N), but does NOT reduce scan loss. Scan loss arises from geometry (projected aperture shrinks as cos(theta)) and physics (mutual coupling changes impedance). These are per-element, scale-independent effects. A 10,000-element array has the same scan loss at 60 degrees as a 100-element array. Practical limits on element count include: physical panel size (each element needs lambda/2 space), power consumption (each element has active T/R modules), thermal dissipation (more elements = more heat in the vacuum of space), weight (launch cost scales with mass), and manufacturing cost.

Q3: The element spacing is lambda/2 = 12.5 mm. If you reduced the spacing to lambda/3, what would happen to: (a) grating lobes, (b) mutual coupling, (c) element gain?

(a) Grating lobes would be pushed further out of the visible region -- the array could scan to greater angles without grating lobes appearing. The grating lobe formula gives sin(theta_g) = sin(theta_s) + lambda/d = sin(theta_s) + 3, which never satisfies |sin| <= 1. (b) Mutual coupling would increase because the elements are physically closer. This means active impedance would vary more with scan, potentially increasing mismatch loss. (c) Element gain would decrease because each patch has a smaller physical area. You would need more elements to achieve the same total gain. In practice, lambda/2 balances these trade-offs optimally for most applications.

Q4: During a satellite pass, the steering angle swings from 65 degrees (AOS) to 5 degrees (TCA) and back to 65 degrees (LOS). Estimate the scan loss variation and explain its impact on the link.

At 65 degrees: cos(65) = 0.423, projected aperture loss = 10*log10(0.423) = -3.74 dB, plus ~0.5 dB mismatch, total scan loss is approximately -4.2 dB. At 5 degrees: cos(5) = 0.996, scan loss is essentially 0 dB. The swing is about 4.2 dB through the pass. Combined with slant range variation (another ~6 dB from path loss), the total geometric signal swing from AOS to TCA is over 10 dB. The adaptive modulation system must respond to this by selecting higher-order modulation at TCA (when SNR is high) and falling back to robust QPSK near AOS/LOS (when SNR is low). If the modulation cannot adapt fast enough, throughput is left on the table.

Q5: Explain the difference between "scan loss" and "element pattern rolloff." Both reduce gain off-boresight -- are they the same thing?

No, they are distinct. Element pattern rolloff (cos^q(theta) from Section 2) describes how a single element's gain varies with angle. It is already included in the G_element term of the array gain equation. Scan loss (Section 5) describes additional losses that arise specifically because the array is steering -- projected aperture reduction and impedance mismatch. In the simulator code, element gain and scan loss are computed by separate functions and added independently. The total effective gain includes both: G_eff = [10*log10(N) + G_element(theta) + taper] + scanLoss(theta). Note that G_element already accounts for the element's own cos^q pattern, while scanLoss adds the array-level geometric and impedance effects on top.

Q6: A satellite has 120 of 1200 elements failed (10%). The link budget has 3 dB margin at the elevation mask. (a) What is the gain loss? (b) At what failure percentage does the link fail at worst case? (c) Why is the actual answer lower than the math suggests?

(a) Delta G = 10*log10(1080/1200) = 10*log10(0.9) = -0.46 dB. Remaining margin: 3.0 - 0.46 = 2.54 dB. (b) Setting 10*log10((1200-M)/1200) = -3.0 gives M = 600, or 50%. (c) The 50% figure is unrealistically high because the 3 dB margin is not reserved solely for element failures. The margin must also cover atmospheric fading, thermal derating (PA gain drops at high temperature), oscillator frequency drift, and other degradation modes. In practice, if you allocate 1 dB of the 3 dB margin to element failures, the limit is 10*log10((1200-M)/1200) = -1.0, giving M = 276, or about 23%. Even this is conservative because failed elements also raise sidelobes, increasing interference.
